# Supplementary Statistical Methods and Results

## Information-Elasticity Coupling (IEC) Framework for Spinal Geometry
### Independent Statistical Reanalysis

---

## 1. Overview of Statistical Approach

All statistical analyses were performed independently on the computational datasets generated by the IEC framework simulations. Analyses were conducted using Python 3.11 with scipy 1.11, scikit-learn 1.3, and numpy 1.24. All tests are two-sided unless otherwise noted. Multiple comparison corrections use Bonferroni method. Effect sizes are reported following Cohen's conventions (small: d=0.2/f²=0.02, medium: d=0.5/f²=0.15, large: d=0.8/f²=0.35).

---

## 2. Cross-Species Allometric Scaling

### 2.1 Primary Analysis
The gravitational buckling parameter Bg was computed for 10 species spanning 5 orders of magnitude in body mass (Mouse: 0.025 kg to Elephant: 5000 kg). Log-log regression of Bg against body mass yields:

**log₁₀(Bg) = −0.282 × log₁₀(Mass) + intercept**

- R² = 0.744, F(1,8) = 23.3, p = 1.31×10⁻³
- Standard error of slope: 0.058
- Cohen's f² = 2.91 (very large effect)

### 2.2 Bootstrap Confidence Intervals
To quantify uncertainty robustly given the small sample (n=10), we performed:

- **Percentile bootstrap** (10,000 resamples): 95% CI = [−0.347, −0.117]
- **BCa bootstrap** (bias-corrected and accelerated): 95% CI = [−0.405, −0.170]
- The metabolic scaling prediction of −0.25 (Kleiber's law) falls **within both CIs**

### 2.3 Hypothesis Testing
- **Permutation test** (10,000 permutations): p = 0.0014, confirming the relationship is not due to chance
- **t-test against metabolic prediction**: t(8) = 0.549, p = 0.603. The observed slope is **not significantly different from −0.25**, consistent with the metabolic scaling hypothesis
- **Leave-one-out cross-validation**: R²_LOO = 0.617, overfitting index = 0.127 (acceptable)

### 2.4 Bayesian Model Comparison
Comparing the IEC allometric model against a null model (Bg independent of mass):
- ΔBIC = 11.34 (very strong evidence per Kass & Raftery 1995 criteria)
- Approximate Bayes Factor ≈ 290 (overwhelming evidence for the scaling relationship)
- ΔAIC = 11.01

## 3. Energy Deficit and Bifurcation Analysis

### 3.1 Energy Deficit Window
Pearson correlation between spine length (L) and predicted Cobb angle across the growth window:
- r = 0.983, p = 2.74×10⁻²², R² = 0.966 (n = 30 L values from 0.25 to 0.55 m)
- The relationship is effectively deterministic within the model

### 3.2 Logistic Regression for Bifurcation
Binary classification of energy deficit state using (χ_κ, L) predictors:
- Accuracy = 78.25%, AUC = 0.978 (excellent discrimination)
- L dominates prediction (coefficient 5.51 vs χ_κ coefficient 0.88)

### 3.3 Phase Transition Detection
Critical spine length L_crit varies with χ_κ:
- At χ_κ = 0.01: L_crit = 0.10 m
- At χ_κ = 0.10: L_crit = 0.25 m
- At χ_κ = 0.20: L_crit = 0.40 m
This defines a continuous bifurcation surface in (χ_κ, L) parameter space.

## 4. Anisotropy Rescue Mechanism

Linear regression of tissue anisotropy (range 1.0–8.0) against critical spine length:
- Slope = −0.085 m per unit anisotropy
- R² = 0.775, p = 3.58×10⁻¹⁷ (n = 51)
- Cohen's f² = 3.44 (very large effect)
- **Saturation threshold**: At anisotropy ≥ 6.0, L_crit plateaus at ~0.20 m

Clinical implication: Connective tissue disorders with reduced anisotropy (e.g., Marfan syndrome, anisotropy ≈ 1.0) have L_crit ≈ 0.80 m, meaning virtually the entire growth trajectory is in the vulnerable zone. This is consistent with the 63% scoliosis prevalence in Marfan patients (Sponseller et al. 1995).

## 5. Circadian Disruption (Spinal Jetlag)

### 5.1 Omnibus Test
Kruskal-Wallis H test across 8 circadian conditions: H = 48.66, p < 10⁻⁶

### 5.2 Key Pairwise Comparison
High-chi jetlag vs. entrained baseline:
- Mean Cobb angle: 24.18° vs 9.61° (2.52-fold increase)
- Mann-Whitney U, p = 2.59×10⁻⁴
- Cohen's d = 1.39 (very large effect)
- 95% CI of mean difference: [6.23°, 22.92°]
- Maximum Cobb under high-chi jetlag: 44.84° (surgical threshold)

### 5.3 Bonferroni-Corrected Post-Hoc Tests
Of 28 pairwise comparisons, 8 survive Bonferroni correction at α = 0.05. All significant pairs involve the high_chi_jetlag condition, confirming it is the sole condition producing clinically meaningful Cobb angle increases.

## 6. Vector Mismatch Localization

Peak information–stress vector mismatch occurs at normalized spine position 0.596, corresponding anatomically to the T11–T12 vertebral level (thoracolumbar junction). At this location:
- Healthy lateral stiffness: 0.725
- Scoliotic lateral stiffness: 0.500
- **Stiffness reduction: 31.1%**

This matches the clinical observation that thoracolumbar curves are the second most common AIS pattern (Lenke classification), and the thoracolumbar junction is known to be biomechanically vulnerable due to the rib cage–lumbar transition.

## 7. Protein Structural Analysis

### 7.1 Term Assignments
22 proteins mapped to 3 IEC terms:
- η_p (proprioceptive/sensory): 5 proteins, mean anisotropy 3.22, mean pLDDT 67.6
- η_a (actuation/muscular): 7 proteins, mean anisotropy 3.42, mean pLDDT 73.1
- Γ_m (metabolic supply): 10 proteins, mean anisotropy 2.47, mean pLDDT 66.3

### 7.2 Sensitivity Analysis (Confidence Stratification)
Of 22 proteins, 11 have high AlphaFold confidence (pLDDT ≥ 70) and 11 have low confidence:
- Anisotropy distributions are **not significantly different** between groups (Mann-Whitney p = 0.844)
- This means the structural predictions are robust to AlphaFold confidence issues
- Term assignments remain unchanged when low-confidence proteins are excluded

### 7.3 Thermogenesis Protein Clustering
All 5 thermogenesis-linked proteins (PPARGC1A, ARNTL, SIRT1, GHR, IGF1R) map to Γ_m:
- Higher mean disorder fraction (0.42) than other terms (0.22–0.25)
- Consistent with their role as signaling hubs (intrinsically disordered proteins are known scaffolding/hub proteins)
