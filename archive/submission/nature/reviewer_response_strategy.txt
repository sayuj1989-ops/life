**Critique 2: "The Cosserat rod model is too simplified. The spine is a segmented structure with muscles, ligaments, and discs. Treating it as a continuous beam is invalid."**

*   **Pre-emptive Rebuttal (for Methods/Discussion):**
    *   "We acknowledge that the spine is a discrete multi-body system. However, for long-wavelength deformations like the global S-curve (wavelength $\lambda \sim L$), the continuum approximation is rigorously valid (homogenization theory). Our 'Effective Stiffness' $B_{\mathrm{eff}}(s)$ captures the composite contribution of discs, ligaments, and muscles. Moreover, adding discrete complexity (e.g., Finite Element Analysis) often obscures the fundamental scaling laws ($L^4$ vs $L^2$) that drive the instability. Our goal is to identify the *minimal* physical ingredients for counter-curvature, not to reproduce every anatomical detail."

**Critique 4: "Why does the 'Gravity Paradox' ($L^4$ vs $L^2$) apply specifically to humans? Other large animals grow rapidly."**

*   **Pre-emptive Rebuttal (for Discussion/Evolution):**
    *   "Quadrupeds load the spine primarily in bending (cantilever) or tension (suspension bridge), where gravitational moments are distributed along multiple supports. Humans, as obligate bipeds, load the spine as an inverted pendulum/column. The critical buckling load for a column scales as $L^{-2}$, making it uniquely sensitive to height increases. Furthermore, the human spine must actively generate a *counter-moment* to maintain lordosis, a demand absent in the C-shaped spines of quadrupeds. This 'Active Moment' cost is what scales as $L^4$, creating the specific thermodynamic bottleneck for our species."
