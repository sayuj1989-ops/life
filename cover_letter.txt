We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "Metabolic buckling of the growing spine," for consideration as an Article in Nature.

Living systems must actively maintain structural integrity against gravity, yet the physical cost of this victory remains unquantified. We present a first-principles derivation showing that the human spine is a "Thermodynamic Standing Wave"—a dissipative structure that inevitably buckles when metabolic supply fails to scale with the L^4 demand of straightening against a gravitational field.

Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) affects 3% of the global population and remains a "medical mystery" despite decades of genomic research. We demonstrate that AIS is not a random bone disease, but a predictable "metabolic buckling" event. Using a novel Information-Cosserat framework, we identify a critical "Energy Deficit Window" (L > 0.35m) where the thermodynamic cost of high-fidelity mechanosensing outstrips metabolic capacity.

Our structural analysis reveals a fatal flaw in vertebrate design: the very proteins required to sense gravity (e.g., Vimentin, PIEZO2) are the most thermodynamically expensive to maintain. We show that "Vector-Scalar Mismatch"—the decoupling of expensive stiffness sensing from cheap volumetric growth—explains the specific vulnerability of the adolescent growth spurt.

This work bridges theoretical physics, structural biology, and clinical medicine. It is not a specialist orthopedic study; rather, it reveals a fundamental scaling law of vertebrate morphogenesis that redefines physical deformity as a strategy for thermodynamic survival.
