I am pleased to submit our manuscript, "Metabolic buckling of the growing spine," for consideration as an Article in Nature.

The grand challenge in vertebrate development is maintaining a metastable, energy-intensive geometry against gravity during rapid volumetric growth. Our manuscript solves a long-standing mystery in developmental biomechanics: we identify that Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) is not a random genetic anomaly or purely mechanical failure, but a predictable "Metabolic Buckling" event. We mathematically demonstrate that the required energy to maintain countercurvature scales as length to the fourth power ($L^4$), while metabolic supply scales only as surface area ($L^2$). This reveals a critical "Energy Deficit Window" during the adolescent growth spurt, forcing the down-regulation of thermodynamically expensive, highly anisotropic mechanosensors (like Vimentin) and triggering a supercritical bifurcation into a scoliotic spiral.

While the clinical manifestation is orthopedic, this work is not a specialist paper on spinal surgery. It exposes a fundamental biological principle regarding the thermodynamic limits of active inference and mechanotransduction in living systems. By mapping the exact protein cost landscape, we show that modifying cytoskeletal anisotropy delays this critical buckling by 60 cm of growth, opening a completely novel, non-mechanical therapeutic window.
