\begin{abstract}
\noindent\textbf{Purpose:} Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) clusters at peak height velocity, in the thoracic spine, and overwhelmingly in girls (8:1), yet no single framework unifies its timing, location, and sex predominance. We test whether AIS can be modelled as failure of an active, gravity-loaded postural-control system.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:} We model the developing spine as a biomechanical control system: a Cosserat rod whose segment-wise stiffness is set by developmental HOX-domain patterning and stabilised against gravity by proprioceptive feedback. Four research strands: (i) cross-species allometric analysis across 12 vertebrates (mouse to dolphin) using $\mathcal{B}_g = EI/MgL^2$; (ii) AlphaFold-derived structural metrics (elongation, hinge-density per 100 residues; length-matched controls) for 61 candidate proteins; (iii) a delay-differential postural-control model through the growth spurt; (iv) clinical cohort validation ($N=1,000$) and bracing simulation compared against the Lonstein \& Carlson progression index and the BrAIST clinical trial.

\noindent\textbf{Results:} Cross-species, only humans operate at $\mathcal{B}_g \approx 0.01$, where passive bending stiffness is insufficient and active proprioceptive maintenance is mandatory. A curated subset of extended-filament/channel mechanosensors (Vimentin, Lamin~A/C, PIEZO2, SCRIB, PTK7) is 72\% more structurally elongated than metabolic regulators ($p=0.011$, Cohen's $d=1.19$, $n=23$; signal dilutes with broader gene sets) and has 70\% fewer hinge regions per residue ($p=0.003$, $n=61$) --- consistent with cooperative load-sensing. In the postural-control simulation, proprioceptive delay scales with body length while neural conduction velocity does not; the resulting feedback-delay instability closes the simulated control margin at the age window of AIS onset, and the model-derived energy deficit correlates strongly with simulated Cobb-angle severity across the growth window ($r=0.983$, $p=2.7\times 10^{-22}$). The effective Bio-Gravitational parameter $B_g^{\mathrm{eff}}$ correlates strongly with the Lonstein \& Carlson Progression Factor ($r = -0.483$, $p < 10^{-50}$). Simulated bracing (modeled as 37\% gravitational load-sharing) replicates the overall success rates of the BrAIST trial (72\% braced vs 48\% observation success) and matches subgroup trends. A linear regression of simulated tissue anisotropy against the critical spine length $L_{\mathrm{crit}}$ shows protective scaling ($R^2=0.775$, $p<10^{-17}$), saturating at anisotropy $\geq 6$.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions:} The framework reframes AIS as a predictable failure of an active biomechanical control system, integrating developmental patterning, proprioceptive feedback, and tissue mechanics. The results show that the Bio-Gravitational parameter serves as a robust biomechanical proxy for clinical progression and brace-treatment efficacy. Three hypothesis-generating predictions follow, presented \emph{not} as clinical recommendations: (i) proprioceptive latency should rise non-linearly through the growth spurt in at-risk adolescents; (ii) the model predicts that mitochondrial NAD$^+$ availability modulates simulated curve progression --- \textbf{this does not endorse nutraceutical use outside an IRB-approved trial}; (iii) ciliary length and cellular dithering should increase in scoliotic osteoblasts before macroscopic onset.
\end{abstract}
