\begin{abstract}
Living systems routinely maintain structure against gravity, yet the physical principle underlying this ability remains unexplained. We develop a quantitative framework---\emph{biological countercurvature}---interpreting this as information-driven modification of effective geometry in a gravitational field. We combine an information--elasticity coupling (IEC) model with three-dimensional Cosserat rod mechanics, defining a biological metric $d\ell_{\mathrm{eff}}^{2} = g_{\mathrm{eff}}(s)\,ds^{2}$ and a normalized geodesic deviation $\widehat{D}_{\mathrm{geo}}$ that separates gravity-dominated, cooperative, and information-dominated regimes. AlphaFold structural analysis of 23 proteins underlying the spinal free energy dissipation functional reveals a 34\% Demand--Supply Anisotropy Gap: demand-side mechanosensors (mean anisotropy 3.32) are inherently more expensive to maintain than supply-side metabolic regulators (mean anisotropy 2.48), creating a thermodynamic vulnerability during rapid adolescent growth when metabolic demand increases by 65--115\% while supply increases by only 34\%. We show that: (i) rapid adolescent growth is an evolved strategy to minimize time in the high-deficit zone rather than a design flaw; (ii) different scoliosis curve patterns correspond to distinct eigenmodes of the coupled system selected by regional protein expression gradients; (iii) the 10:1 female preponderance arises from a deeper metabolic deficit (narrower but more severe Energy Deficit Window) rather than structural weakness; and (iv) the energy supply failure involves six molecular mechanisms beyond simple hunger, including a mitochondrial capacity ceiling, vascular limitation, circadian desynchrony, and a recursive supply constraint. Small asymmetries in the information field are amplified into scoliosis-like deformities only within a specific thermodynamic regime, providing a unified geometric and metabolic explanation for adolescent idiopathic scoliosis.
\end{abstract}
