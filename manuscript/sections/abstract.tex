
\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} The human spine maintains a complex S-shaped sagittal profile against gravity, yet its physical principle and adolescent vulnerability remain unexplained. We propose that Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) represents a predictable "Metabolic Buckling" event, modeled through mathematical control theory.

\textbf{Methods:} We introduce the Bio-Gravitational Number $\mathcal{B}_g = EI/(MgL^2)$, analogous to the Froude number, to quantify when shape maintenance becomes metabolically dependent. The spine is formalized as an active control system governed by a Delay Differential Equation (DDE). We evaluate how the absolute neural feedback delay $\tau$ increases during the adolescent growth spurt.

\textbf{Results:} Cross-species analysis confirms humans uniquely occupy an ``Allometric Trap'' ($\mathcal{B}_g \approx 0.01$). As the adolescent growth spurt increases the neural delay $\tau$, the system undergoes a supercritical Hopf bifurcation. Derivative gains that provide essential damping during childhood become destabilizing, creating a "Derivative Gain Trap" that initiates scoliotic buckling. We hypothesize this is exacerbated by a transient Energy Deficit Window, driven by high $L^4$ geometric scaling costs vs $L^2$ metabolic supply, leading to localized NAD+ insufficiency.

\textbf{Conclusions:} These results reclassify AIS as a thermodynamic scaling failure and delay-induced instability, bridging mathematical control theory and clinical onset timing. We identify measurable predictions for preventing progression via metabolic intervention rather than purely mechanical bracing.
\end{abstract}
