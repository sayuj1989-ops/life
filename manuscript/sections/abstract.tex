\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} The human spine maintains a complex S-shaped sagittal profile against gravity, yet the physical principle underlying this counter-curvature and its adolescent vulnerability remains unexplained.
\textbf{Methods:} We introduce the Information–Elasticity Coupling (IEC) framework, integrating a developmental information field $I(s)$ with Cosserat rod mechanics. We perform 3D simulations using PyElastica and analyze AlphaFold structures of 23 mechanotransduction proteins.
\textbf{Results:} IEC selects the S-shaped profile as the energetic ground state, reproducing clinical lordosis ($\sim$42$^\circ$) and kyphosis ($\sim$35$^\circ$). Phase diagrams reveal a stable cooperative regime and a scoliosis-prone information-dominated regime. During simulated growth, the ``Energy Deficit Window'' opens at a critical length $L_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.35$~m, where metabolic demand ($\propto L^3$) outpaces supply ($\propto L^2$). Small asymmetries amplify into scoliotic deformities via supercritical bifurcation. Structural analysis reveals a 34\% demand–supply anisotropy gap, quantifying the molecular cost of correcting gravitational errors.
\textbf{Conclusions:} Spinal geometry emerges as a thermodynamic standing wave. IEC unifies patterning and biomechanics, explaining idiopathic scoliosis onset and predicting specific molecular vulnerabilities.
\end{abstract}
