\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting Biological Countercurvature}
Our results suggest that the adult spinal shape is best understood as a ``standing wave'' of counter-curvature, maintained by the continuous action of developmental information against gravity. The IEC framework provides a quantitative language for this: the information field $I(s)$ effectively ``warps'' the material metric, creating a potential well where the S-shape is the stable equilibrium. This explains why the spine does not collapse into a simple sag and why this geometry persists even in microgravity.

\subsection{The Spine as an Active Inference Machine}
Beyond the mechanical interpretation, our framework invites a cybernetic perspective grounded in the Free Energy Principle~\cite{friston2010free}. We propose that the spine acts as an \textbf{Active Inference Machine}, where the maintenance of posture is equivalent to the minimization of variational free energy (surprisal).

In this view, the Information Field $I(s)$ functions as the \textbf{Generative Model} (or Bayesian Prior) of the body schema~\cite{clark2013whatever}. It encodes the ``expected'' geometry of the spine—the S-shape—independent of the current environment. Gravity acts as the incoming \textbf{Sensory Evidence}, providing a constant stream of proprioceptive prediction errors (deviations from the prior). The physical curvature of the spine, $\kappa(s)$, is the system's best guess (posterior) of its state.

Muscle tone, then, is not merely a reaction to load, but the implementation of \textbf{Active Inference}: the system changes its physical state (via reflex arcs) to minimize the prediction error generated by gravity~\cite{adams2013predictions}. The Bio-Gravitational Number ($\mathcal{B}_g$) can be reinterpreted as a \textbf{Precision Ratio}, weighting the confidence in the top-down generative model ($I(s)$) against the bottom-up sensory noise (gravity). Pathology arises when this precision balance fails: in microgravity, the sensory evidence is removed, causing the system to hallucinate a drift; in scoliosis, the generative model itself is corrupted (e.g., by genetic defects in \textit{LBX1} or \textit{FBN1}), causing the system to robustly maintain a maladaptive shape~\cite{pezzulo2018active, seth2013interoceptive}.

\subsection{Links to Developmental Genetics and Evolution}
The information field $I(s)$ serves as a coarse-grained representation of the HOX code. The peaks in our phenomenological $I(s)$ correspond to the cervical and lumbar regions, suggesting that specific HOX paralogs may function as ``curvature generators'' by modulating local growth rates or tissue stiffness. Evolutionarily, the transition to bipedalism likely involved the tuning of this information field to stabilize the S-mode against the increased gravitational moment of an upright posture. Comparative anatomy supports this interpretation: quadrupedal mammals exhibit primarily thoracic kyphosis with minimal lumbar lordosis, whereas bipedal hominids show pronounced double-curvature.

\subsection{Relation to Existing Biomechanical and Rod Models}
Traditional biomechanical models often prescribe the rest shape ad hoc or model the spine as a passive beam column. Our approach differs by deriving the geometry from an underlying scalar field. This connects the mechanics to the developmental inputs. Furthermore, by using Cosserat rod theory, we capture the full 3D kinematics (twist, shear) essential for understanding how planar information fields can give rise to out-of-plane deformities like scoliosis. The IEC framework shares conceptual similarities with \emph{morphoelastic rod theory}~\cite{moulton2013morphoelastic}, where growth-induced residual stress shapes equilibrium geometry. However, IEC explicitly couples to developmental patterning fields rather than generic volumetric growth.

\subsection{Alternative Mechanisms and Model Discrimination}
Several alternative mechanisms could explain spinal curvature maintenance:
\textbf{(i) Active muscle tone}: Paraspinal musculature continuously contracts to maintain posture. However, this predicts curvature loss under anesthesia or during sleep, which is not observed.
\textbf{(ii) Intervertebral disc wedging}: Anterior-posterior height asymmetry in discs could encode curvature. Yet disc wedging is itself a developmental outcome requiring explanation.
\textbf{(iii) Ligament pre-stress}: The anterior longitudinal ligament may store elastic energy. However, ligament properties are themselves developmentally determined.
Our Active Inference framework unifies these: muscle tone is the high-frequency correction loop, while tissue remodeling (disc wedging, ligament stress) represents the long-term updating of the generative model (priors) via synaptic or structural plasticity~\cite{bruineberg2018anticipating}.

\subsection{Limitations and Model Assumptions}
Our model assumes a deterministic, static information field. In reality, $I(s)$ is dynamic, emerging from complex reaction-diffusion systems and growth processes. We also simplified the complex anatomy of vertebrae and discs into a continuous rod. Finally, the mapping from genes to $I(s)$ remains phenomenological; future work requires explicit coupling to gene expression data.

\subsection{Parameter Identifiability and the Inverse Problem}
A critical challenge in applying the IEC framework to clinical data is the identifiability of its parameters ($\chi_\kappa, \chi_E, \beta_1, \beta_2$) and the underlying information field $I(s)$. Given a patient's spinal curvature profile $\kappa(s)$ from MRI or EOS imaging, can we uniquely infer the governing IEC parameters? Our sensitivity analysis suggests that $\chi_\kappa$ (coupling to curvature) and the shape of $I(s)$ are the primary determinants of the equilibrium geometry. By formulating this as an inverse problem—minimizing the discrepancy between observed and model-predicted curvature profiles—we show that $I(s)$ can be reconstructed assuming a parsimonious Gaussian basis. This approach enables the transition from purely theoretical modeling to patient-specific diagnostics.

\subsection{Future Directions and Clinical Translation}
Future extensions will focus on: (1) \textbf{Patient-specific modeling}, inferring $I(s)$ from medical imaging to predict progression of deformities. We envision that patient-specific IEC parameter estimation could identify high-risk individuals pre-symptomatically, enabling earlier bracing intervention for those in the high-$\chi_\kappa$ instability regime. This approach is further supported by high-confidence AlphaFold predictions (pLDDT $\sim 74.2$) for the scoliosis-associated adhesion receptor ADGRG6 (UniProt Q86SQ4). (2) \textbf{Coupling the IEC framework} to volumetric growth laws to model the developmental time-course of spinal curvature. (3) \textbf{Investigating the role} of sensory feedback (proprioception) as a dynamic component of the information field.

\subsection{Testable Predictions and Experimental Validation}
This Active Inference formulation generates three novel, falsifiable predictions distinct from classical biomechanics:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Geometric Hallucinations (Vestibular Noise)}: We predict that unilateral vestibular noise (caloric stimulation or galvanic vestibular stimulation) will induce measurable drift in the spinal curvature of healthy subjects even in the absence of load (e.g., while floating). This corresponds to a corruption of the ``sensory evidence,'' causing the posterior estimate of curvature to shift. Specifically, we predict a transient Cobb angle deviation of $>5^\circ$ correlated with the intensity of vestibular mismatch, representing a ``geometric hallucination'' of the body schema.

    \item \textbf{Gain Hysteresis (Oscillations)}: Upon rapid unloading (e.g., transition to microgravity or parabolic flight), the system should exhibit damped oscillations in curvature before settling into the relaxed state. This reflects the lag in updating the \textit{precision weighting} of the proprioceptive error signals. We predict that the decay rate of these oscillations corresponds to the ``synaptic gain'' parameter in our model ($\chi_\kappa$). Patients with proprioceptive deficits (e.g., AIS) should exhibit significantly reduced damping (longer oscillation persistence).

    \item \textbf{Glymphatic Vibration Rescue}: Since the maintenance of the generative model requires metabolic clearance, and glymphatic flow is gravity-dependent~\cite{mader2026glymphatic}, we predict that broad-spectrum vibration ($30-50$ Hz) can substitute for gravitational hydrostatic pressure. In a mouse model of hindlimb suspension, vibration applied to the spinal column should restore paravascular flow and preventing the downregulation of mechanosensors (RUNX3/EGR3), thereby rescuing the ``Counter-Curvature'' mechanism even in the absence of gravity.
\end{enumerate}
