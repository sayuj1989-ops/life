\section{Introduction}

Living organisms routinely maintain complex, non-equilibrium shapes against the relentless pull of gravity. A tree grows vertically despite wind loads; a flamingo stands on one leg; the human spine sustains an intricate S-shaped profile (lordosis and kyphosis) rather than sagging into the passive C-curve predicted by beam mechanics~\cite{thompson1917growth, goriely2017mathematics}. The physical principle that governs this ``anti-gravitational form maintenance'' --- and its limits --- remains poorly understood.

For passive structures, the mechanics are straightforward: a beam clamped at one end sags monotonically under its own weight. Yet the vertebrate spine actively opposes this gravitational geodesic, maintaining a counter-curved geometry that is energetically expensive~\cite{latimer2005upright}. This maintenance requires continuous metabolic expenditure through proprioceptive feedback and muscular actuation, governed by the same gravitropic control laws observed across phyla~\cite{bastien2013unifying, moulia2009gravitropic}. The spine is, in this sense, a \emph{dissipative structure}: a far-from-equilibrium configuration sustained by the continuous investment of free energy~\cite{prigogine1977self}.

A fundamental scaling crisis emerges during growth. As the spine elongates, the gravitational bending moment scales as $M_g \propto L^4$~\cite{oconnell2007scaling}, while the nutrient supply to the avascular intervertebral disc --- constrained by vertebral endplate surface area --- scales only as $L^2$~\cite{urban2004nutrition}. We formalize this divergence through the \textbf{Bio-Gravitational Number}, $\mathcal{B}_g = EI / (M g L^2)$, a dimensionless ratio of passive flexural stiffness to gravitational moment. Cross-species analysis reveals that most vertebrates maintain $\mathcal{B}_g > 0.1$ (passively stable), but humans occupy a unique ``Allometric Trap'' ($\mathcal{B}_g \approx 0.01$) where passive stiffness is insufficient and active metabolic maintenance is required.

This scaling mismatch creates a transient \textbf{Energy Deficit Window} during the adolescent growth spurt, when the metabolic cost of maintaining the S-shaped counter-curvature ($\propto L^2$) outpaces the biological supply capacity ($\propto L^{0.5}$). Beyond a critical spinal length $L_{\mathrm{crit}} \approx 0.35$\,m, the system becomes energetically insolvent. We propose that Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) --- a 3D spinal deformity affecting 3\% of children worldwide --- is the predictable consequence of this thermodynamic insolvency: a ``metabolic buckling'' event in which the spine sheds its expensive counter-curvature and collapses into a lower-energy, asymmetric mode.

Here we develop an Information--Elasticity Coupling (IEC) framework that connects developmental patterning (HOX gene expression) to Cosserat rod mechanics via a scalar information field $I(s)$. We show that: (i) the IEC framework selects the spinal S-curve as the energetic ground state, reproducing clinically normal lordosis ($\sim 42^\circ$) and kyphosis ($\sim 35^\circ$) from first principles; (ii) cross-species analysis of the Bio-Gravitational Number confirms a universal scaling threshold separating passively stable from metabolically dependent body plans; (iii) AlphaFold structural analysis of 23 key proteins reveals a 72\% demand--supply anisotropy gap ($p = 0.011$), providing molecular-resolution evidence for the metabolic vulnerability; and (iv) the framework generates six specific, quantitative, falsifiable predictions spanning genetics, microgravity physiology, and clinical biomechanics.
