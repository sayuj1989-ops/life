\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background: The Gravity--Biology Paradox}
Life on Earth has evolved within the unyielding context of a constant gravitational field. For over three billion years, this force ($g \approx 9.81$ m/s$^2$) has acted as a ubiquitous morphogenetic cue, shaping everything from the cytoskeleton of individual cells to the skeletal architecture of large vertebrates~\cite{thompson1917growth,morey2002physiological}. For sessile or passive structures, gravity dictates a simple, low-energy equilibrium: a beam clamped at one end and subject to its own weight will inevitably sag into a monotonic C-shape~\cite{goriely2017mathematics,moulton2013morphoelastic}. This passive tendency is opposed by biological growth and remodeling, which operate under the constraints of ``morphomechanics''---the interplay between mechanical stress and developmental patterning~\cite{taber1995biomechanics,beloussov1998morphomechanics}.

In vertebrates, particularly bipedal humans, the challenge is acute. The human spine must support the head and trunk against gravity while enabling locomotion. Instead of sagging into a passive C-shape, the healthy human spine maintains a complex, robust S-shaped profile. This geometry is encoded in the HOX gene clusters~\cite{wellik2007hox,burke1995hox}, but the ``Translation Problem'' remains: how is a discrete genetic code translated into continuous analog curvature? Evidence from spaceflight shows that the S-shape persists even in microgravity~\cite{green2018spinal,clement2025adaptation}, suggesting it is an actively maintained ``counter-curvature''—a geometric standing wave driven by biological information against the passive gravitational metric.

\subsection{Mechanotransduction and the "Sensor Mismatch" Problem}
How does living tissue ``know'' what shape to adopt? The field of mechanobiology has revealed that cells act as "architects," probing substrate stiffness via Piezo channels~\cite{coste2010piezo, ranade2014piezo2} and YAP/TAZ signaling~\cite{dupont2011role}.

However, a fundamental gap remains: what is the "set-point"? We propose that in microgravity, the system faces a \textbf{Vector-Scalar Mismatch}. We distinguish between:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vector Sensors} (e.g., Piezo2, Lamin A/C): Anisotropic structures detecting alignment and tension (the ``Direction'' of gravity)~\cite{ranade2014piezo2, swift2013nuclear}.
    \item \textbf{Scalar Sensors} (e.g., Piezo1, SLYM): Isotropic sensors detecting hydrostatic pressure or density (the ``Magnitude'' of stress)~\cite{sun2019piezo1, ramli2024piezo1}.
\end{enumerate}
In microgravity, Vector Sensors go silent (loss of tension), while Scalar Sensors remain active (osmotic swelling). This sensory dissociation blinds the system, leading to homeostatic drift~\cite{touchstone2019recovery, wuest2025vim} and the ``fatty infiltration'' of paraspinal muscles~\cite{shao2025ros, handschin2024paraspinal}.

\subsection{The Spine as an Active Inference Machine}
To explain the robustness of spinal geometry, we propose a unifying hypothesis rooted in the Free Energy Principle~\cite{friston2010free}: the spine acts as an **Active Inference Machine**.

\subsubsection{Gravity as Sensory Evidence}
In this framework, the spine constantly minimizes a Free Energy functional ($U_{CC}$), representing the prediction error between its **Generative Model** (the Genetic Reference Metric, $\bar{g}$) and the **Sensory Evidence** (the Actual Metric, $g$, deformed by gravity). Gravity is not merely a load; it is the primary ``Interrogator'' that constantly tests the system's structural predictions.

The observed S-shape is the system's **Posterior Belief**: the optimal compromise that balances the ``Passive Gravitational Prior'' (the tendency to sag) with the ``Active Biological Prior'' (the genetic instruction to stay upright)~\cite{pezzulo2018active, adams2013predictions}.

\subsubsection{Spinal Jetlag and the Learning Rate}
We propose that the Circadian Clock (BMAL1) acts as a ``Learning Rate Scheduler'' for this inference process. The Intervertebral Disc (IVD) clock is entrained by the daily cycle of gravitational loading~\cite{dudek2017clock, yang2017circadian}. In the absence of this ``Gravitational Zeitgeber'' (e.g., in spaceflight), the system suffers from **Spinal Jetlag**, leading to a desynchronization of tissue clocks and a downregulation of proprioceptive gain (RUNX3/EGR3)~\cite{blecher2017new}.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}
This framework yields specific, falsifiable predictions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Vestibular Precision Ratio ($\Omega_{VP}$)}: We predict that Idiopathic Scoliosis is a ``Geometric Hallucination'' caused by high sensory noise. Patients will exhibit a low Vestibular Precision Ratio (high variance in otolith signals). This can be tested via Stochastic Vestibular Stimulation (SVS); we predict that adding noise via SVS will paradoxically \textit{improve} EMG symmetry in scoliosis patients by forcing the CNS to re-weight proprioceptive priors~\cite{pialasse2015vestibular, manzoni2002vestibular}.
    \item \textbf{The Glymphatic-MMP Axis}: We predict that spinal glymphatic clearance is gravity-assisted. Unloading leads to stasis in the paravascular pathway~\cite{iliff2012glymphatic, mader2026glymphatic}, causing local accumulation of waste. This triggers Matrix Metalloproteinases (MMP1/MMP3) before structural degeneration occurs. This can be validated by measuring tracer clearance in microgravity analogs~\cite{chen2025mmp}.
    \item \textbf{The Adipogenic Switch}: We posit that ``fatty infiltration'' in unloaded muscle is an active metabolic switch gated by mitochondrial ROS. We predict that scavenging ROS (e.g., via MitoQ) will prevent adipogenesis even in the absence of mechanical loading, decoupling atrophy from fatty infiltration~\cite{shao2025ros, handschin2024paraspinal}.
\end{enumerate}

In the following sections, we formalize this theory using Cosserat rod mechanics~\cite{gazzola2018forward}, demonstrating how minimizing $U_{CC}$ naturally emerges the S-shaped spine and how parameter mismatches lead to scoliotic deformities.
