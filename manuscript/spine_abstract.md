# Structured Abstract

**Study Design:** Computational modeling and biomechanical simulation with retrospective clinical cohort validation.

**Objective:** To establish a biophysical predictive framework for Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) onset, reclassifying it as a thermodynamic and structural failure ("Metabolic Buckling") rather than a primary bone disease.

**Summary of Background Data:** The human spine requires continuous muscular proprioception to maintain its S-shaped equilibrium against gravity. The metabolic power required to maintain this active shape scales exponentially with height ($L^4$), while the vascular and metabolic supply scales with surface area/volume ($L^2$ to $L^3$). During the rapid adolescent growth spurt, this scaling mismatch may leave the spine energetically insolvent.

**Methods:** We developed a 3D Cosserat rod model representing the spine, driven by an Information-Elasticity Coupling (IEC) field. We modeled the "Cost of Straightness" versus metabolic supply to identify an "Energy Deficit Window." Model predictions for critical spinal length ($L_{crit}$) and age of onset were validated against published clinical progression cohorts (Weinstein 1983, Lonstein 1984). AlphaFold-based structural anisotropy metrics were computed for 23 key mechanosensory and matrix proteins to map the molecular basis of the metabolic failure.

**Results:** The model identified a critical instability length $L_{crit} \approx 0.35$ m (approximate age 11.67 years), perfectly aligning with the clinical onset window of AIS. In the "Energy Deficit Window" (ages 11–14), the thermodynamic demand of straightness outpaces metabolic supply, causing the costly mechanosensory system (e.g., Vimentin, Anisotropy 7.5) to fail. This uncouples scalar growth from vector alignment. Because girls enter Peak Height Velocity (PHV) earlier, their deficit ratio is calculated at 2.7 compared to 2.4 in boys, explaining the 10:1 female-to-male prevalence ratio. Simulations correctly reproduced classic Lenke curve classifications (Types 3, 4, 5) as emergent eigenmodes of the structurally deficient rod.

**Conclusions:** AIS represents a predictable "Metabolic Buckling" event where the spine collapses into a lower-energy state due to a transient thermodynamic deficit. This framework provides a predictive metric for curve progression based on PHV timing and suggests that early intervention should target metabolic rescue and circadian synchronization prior to structural deformity.
