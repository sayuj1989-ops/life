# Evidence Note: PIEZO2 Duplications and Mechanosensory Neuromuscular Scoliosis

## Claim
Intragenic duplications in the *PIEZO2* gene, which encodes a critical mechanosensitive ion channel, lead to a recessive mechanosensory neuropathy characterized by severe proprioceptive deficits and musculoskeletal abnormalities, prominently including thoracolumbar neuromuscular scoliosis (Lee et al., 2025).

## Mechanism (genes/proteins/pathways)
*PIEZO2* encodes the principal mechanotransducer for proprioception and touch. A homozygous intragenic duplication involving *PIEZO2* exons disrupts the overall protein structure and channel function. This structural disruption impairs the mechanotransduction of mechanical forces (e.g., tension, compression, and shear) into calcium signals within proprioceptive neurons and interoceptive pathways, breaking the primary sensory input necessary for reflex arcs and active muscle tone maintenance.

## Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis
The S-shaped human spine relies heavily on active muscular support to maintain its geometry against gravity, requiring precise and continuous proprioceptive feedback. The loss of PIEZO2 function deprives the paraspinal muscles of the positional and tension feedback needed to correct minor deviations. Without this error-correction signal, the spine succumbs to mechanical instability and buckles under axial load, leading to severe neuromuscular scoliosis, as observed in the patient described by Lee et al. (2025).

## How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis
This directly supports the biological counter-curvature hypothesis, which posits that the spine is an actively controlled structural system where proprioceptive supply (S_proprio) must meet or exceed thermodynamic/mechanical demand (P_counter). PIEZO2 acts as the primary sensory antenna for this system. When *PIEZO2* is mutated or duplicated, the proprioceptive gain ($\gamma$) drops precipitously. The control system is essentially "blinded" to gravitational and postural strain, leading to unchecked buckling (scoliosis).

## Open question + proposed test
**Open Question:** Does a partial loss or altered threshold of PIEZO2 mechanosensitivity (e.g., via heterozygous duplication or hypomorphic variants) scale proportionally with the severity or onset velocity of the spinal curvature during peak growth?

**Proposed Test:** Generate a mouse model with a heterozygous *PIEZO2* intragenic duplication and subject it to longitudinal bi-planar X-ray imaging during the rapid growth phase (P20-P40). Compare the rate of Cobb angle progression and paraspinal EMG asymmetry against wild-type mice, specifically testing if curvature accelerates only when spinal length surpasses a critical buckling threshold that the diminished proprioceptive gain can no longer stabilize.
