# Evidence Note: Tension-Sensitive HOX Gene Expression

**Date:** 2026-02-13
**Author:** Curvature Librarian
**Topic:** Mechanotransduction, HOX Genes, Spinal Morphology

## Claim
Mechanical tension directly modulates HOX gene expression in fibroblasts, linking physical forces to morphogenetic identity.

## Mechanism
Kang et al. (2025) demonstrate that *HOX* genes (specifically those involved in segmental identity and scar formation) are tension-sensitive. Fibroblasts subjected to mechanical tension exhibit differential expression of *HOX* transcripts compared to those in relaxed or low-tension environments. This regulation likely involves cytoskeletal-nuclear coupling (LINC complex) and mechanotransducers like YAP/TAZ, which can access chromatin and potentially interact with *HOX* promoters or enhancers.

## Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis
The "Biological Counter-Curvature" hypothesis posits that the spine's geometry is not just a static structural property but an actively maintained state involving "information fields" coupled to mechanics. *HOX* genes define the vertebral identity (e.g., thoracic vs. lumbar) and, crucially, the local curvature set-point ($\kappa_{rest}$).
If *HOX* expression is tension-sensitive, then:
1.  **Feedback Loop:** Asymmetric loading (scoliosis) creates asymmetric tension.
2.  **Identity Shift:** This tension asymmetry alters local *HOX* expression.
3.  **Morphological Lock:** The altered *HOX* profile drives differential growth or ECM remodeling (scarring/fibrosis) that reinforces the curvature, creating a "vicious cycle" at the genetic level, distinct from the classic Hueter-Volkmann law (which is purely growth-plate based).

This finding provides a direct molecular link between the "Information Field" ($\nabla I$) and the stress tensor ($\boldsymbol{\sigma}$).
