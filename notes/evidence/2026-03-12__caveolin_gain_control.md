# Evidence Note: Caveolin-1 as a Membrane Tension Capacitor and YAP Gatekeeper

**Date:** 2026-03-12
**Reference:** Moreno-Vicente et al. (2018), "Caveolin-1 Modulates Mechanotransduction Responses to Substrate Stiffness through Actin-Dependent Control of YAP", *Cell Reports*.

## Claim
Caveolin-1 (CAV1) functions as a mechanical "Gain Control" element for the YAP/TAZ pathway. By forming invaginated caveolae that flatten under tension, CAV1 buffers the cell membrane against transient mechanical noise. Its loss leads to "Mechanotransductive Hypersensitivity," causing constitutive YAP nuclear translocation and fibrosis even under physiological stiffness conditions.

## Mechanism
1.  **Tension Buffering:** Caveolae act as membrane reservoirs. Under low tension (unloading/microgravity), they are invaginated. Under high tension (loading), they flatten, increasing effective membrane area and buffering stress.
2.  **YAP Inhibition:** CAV1 is required to sequester YAP in the cytoplasm via the actin cytoskeleton. In the absence of CAV1 (or when caveolae are chronically flattened), YAP translocates to the nucleus, driving osteogenesis and matrix stiffening.
3.  **Gain Modulation:** The presence of a "caveolae reserve" reduces the gain of the mechanosensory loop ($\gamma$). Loss of this reserve (due to genetics or chronic unloading) increases $\gamma$, pushing the system towards instability (Bastien's Hopf bifurcation).

## Relevance to Scoliosis / Counter-Curvature
-   **Asymmetric Growth:** If CAV1 expression is heterogeneous across the vertebral growth plate, regions with lower CAV1 will exhibit higher YAP activity and faster growth/stiffening, driving curvature.
-   **Microgravity Paradox:** In microgravity, reduced membrane tension *should* favor caveolae invagination (low YAP). However, if the buffering capacity is lost (e.g., due to atrophy or lipid raft disruption), the cell may paradoxically sense "high stiffness" due to the loss of the damper, leading to the fibrotic/adipogenic switch observed in paraspinal muscles.
-   **Buckling Threshold:** A high-gain system buckles under lower loads. CAV1 deficiency lowers the critical buckling load of the spine by removing the "noise filter" that normally prevents random fluctuations from becoming self-reinforcing curves.

We predict *Cav1*-/- mice will exhibit a higher variance in spinal curvature (Cobb angle) due to the lack of "gain dampening," validating the capacitor hypothesis.
