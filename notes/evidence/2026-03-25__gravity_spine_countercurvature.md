# Evidence Note: Primary Cilia Elongation Impairs Mechanotransduction in AIS

## Claim
Primary cilia in osteoblasts derived from Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) patients exhibit abnormal elongation and impaired mechanotransduction, reducing their ability to accurately translate gravitational and mechanical strains into downstream cellular signaling (Oliazadeh et al., 2017; Oliazadeh et al., 2022).

## Mechanism (genes/proteins/pathways)
The primary cilium functions as a critical cellular "antenna" for mechanosensing, converting extracellular physical forces (such as fluid shear stress induced by loading) into biochemical signals. In AIS, structural elongation of these cilia is associated with a blunted mechanotransductive response. This impaired signaling cascade likely involves well-known mechanosensitive pathways (like YAP/TAZ and PIEZO channels) that normally rely on the precise geometry and resonance of the primary cilium to function correctly.

## Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis
The growing spine depends on constant mechanical feedback, primarily driven by gravity, to maintain symmetric, coordinated growth across vertebral segments (the Hueter-Volkmann principle). If the osteoblasts and other mechanosensitive cells within the spine cannot accurately "sense" this load due to defective cilia, the regulatory feedback loop is broken. The spine effectively "experiences" a localized state of microgravity, losing the stable reference vector required for straight, S-shaped sagittal alignment.

## How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis
This evidence strongly supports the biological counter-curvature hypothesis. The hypothesis posits that spinal alignment is actively maintained by an anti-gravity moment generated in response to accurate sensing of the gravity vector. If the primary mechanosensory organelle—the cilium—is defective, this crucial sensing step is blunted. Without accurate vector information, the system cannot generate the precise counter-curvature needed to resist buckling, leading to the lateral curvature and rotation characteristic of scoliosis as the system mechanically drifts.

## Open question + proposed test
**Open Question:** Does restoring wild-type primary cilia length and function in AIS-derived cells rescue gravity-dependent alignment and mechanosensitive signaling?

**Proposed Test:** Treat AIS patient-derived osteoblasts with cilia-modulating agents (e.g., lithium chloride or specific intraflagellar transport (IFT) inhibitors) to restore normal cilia length. Expose these treated cells, alongside untreated AIS cells and healthy controls, to simulated microgravity (via clinostat) and standard 1g conditions. Measure the recovery of mechanosensitive responses, specifically quantifying YAP/TAZ nuclear translocation and downstream osteogenic gene expression.
