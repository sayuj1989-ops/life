# Evidence Note: PIEZO1-Mediated Ferroptosis in Vertebral Growth Plate Dysplasia

**Date:** $(date +%Y-%m-%d)

## Claim
Mechanical compressive stress on the spine overactivates the PIEZO1 mechanosensitive channel in growth plate chondrocytes. This overactivation suppresses Glutathione Peroxidase 4 (GPX4) activity, inducing lipid peroxidation and ultimately causing ferroptosis (iron-dependent programmed cell death). This targeted cell death in the growth plate leads to asymmetrical ossification, driving Vertebral Growth Plate Dysplasia (VGPD) and scoliosis progression.

## Mechanism (Genes/Proteins/Pathways)
- **PIEZO1:** Mechanosensitive cation channel (highly sensitive to compressive loading and membrane tension).
- **GPX4 (Glutathione Peroxidase 4):** Key antioxidant enzyme that protects cells from lipid peroxidation; its suppression is a hallmark of ferroptosis.
- **Ferroptosis Pathway:** Mechanical force $\rightarrow$ PIEZO1 activation $\rightarrow$ $Ca^{2+}$ influx $\rightarrow$ iron metabolism reprogramming (iron influx/accumulation) $\rightarrow$ GPX4 inhibition $\rightarrow$ ROS accumulation $\rightarrow$ Ferroptosis.

## Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis
The adolescent growth spurt requires symmetrical longitudinal growth of the vertebral bodies. If abnormal mechanical loading (e.g., subtle initial postural deviations or gravitational imbalances) is applied, the high-pressure regions (concave side) experience compressive stress overload. According to the Chen et al. (2025) findings, this stress overactivates PIEZO1, causing localized ferroptosis of the growth plate chondrocytes. The death of these cells arrests growth on the concave side while the convex side continues to grow, creating a vicious cycle of increasing wedge deformation and accelerating the S-shaped scoliotic curve (Hueter-Volkmann principle mediated at the molecular level).

## How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis
**Supports.** The biological counter-curvature hypothesis posits that developmental information (via active sensory feedback like proprioception and mechanotransduction) constructs a "counter-curvature" to resist gravity. When the metabolic cost ($P_{counter}$) exceeds the capacity, or when the sensory gain degrades, the spine buckles. The PIEZO1-GPX4-ferroptosis axis provides a precise, thermodynamic molecular "failure mode" when gravity overwhelms the system. The excessive localized stress resulting from a buckled state (where the counter-curvature fails to perfectly oppose gravity) translates directly into structural destruction (ferroptosis) via PIEZO1 overactivation. It defines the biological penalty for structural misalignment.

## Open question + proposed test
**Open Question:** Does the microgravity environment (which removes compressive stress) protect growth plate chondrocytes from ferroptosis, but cause a secondary failure (e.g., adipogenic shift) due to *under-activation* of PIEZO1?

**Proposed Test:**
Culture adolescent vertebral growth plate explants in a bioreactor under three conditions: (1) 1g control, (2) simulated microgravity (unloading), and (3) hyper-gravity/compression (centrifuge). Measure PIEZO1 activation levels, intracellular iron content, and GPX4 activity across the three groups. We predict that while compression induces ferroptosis (low GPX4, high iron), microgravity will show baseline/high GPX4 but may exhibit down-regulation of essential osteogenic markers downstream of basal PIEZO1 activity, indicating a "goldilocks" zone for PIEZO1 tension in maintaining the counter-curvature.
