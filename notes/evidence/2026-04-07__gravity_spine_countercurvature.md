# Evidence Note: ROCK-dependent Mechanotransduction and Spinal Fibrosis

**Claim**: Chronic repetitive macroscale forces on the spinal column are translated into degenerative fibrosis via a ROCK-dependent mechanotransduction pathway, linking physical loading directly to tissue-level structural changes in spinal disease.

**Mechanism (Genes/Proteins/Pathways)**: The mechanical forces are sensed and transmitted through the Rho-associated protein kinase (ROCK) pathway. Activation of ROCK signaling by macroscale forces alters cellular tension and cytoskeletal dynamics, which in turn drives the activation of fibroblasts and the excessive deposition of fibrotic extracellular matrix (ECM) components.

**Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis**: The healthy S-shaped spine relies on an active, carefully regulated feedback loop where structural tension maintains the correct geometric alignment. Fibrosis, driven by aberrant mechanotransduction (as shown by ROCK dependence), changes the passive material properties ($E$) of the paraspinal tissues and ligaments. This stiffening disrupts the dynamic flexibility needed for active error correction, creating an asymmetric "stiffness trap" that can initiate or lock in scoliotic buckling.

**How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis**: This paper supports the "Biological Countercurvature" hypothesis by demonstrating that physical forces are actively integrated into ECM remodeling via specific kinase pathways. It reinforces the idea that the spine's geometry is not just a passive result of gravity, but an active mechanobiological construct. If the sensing or the response mechanism (like ROCK) is maladaptively triggered by abnormal loads or sensor failure (e.g., in microgravity or scoliosis), the system compensates with pathological fibrosis instead of functional alignment.

**Open question + proposed test**: Does pharmacological inhibition of ROCK (e.g., via Fasudil or Y-27632) prevent the onset or progression of asymmetric paraspinal fibrosis and scoliotic curvature in animal models subjected to mechanically induced spinal buckling (e.g., unilateral compression or tethering)?
