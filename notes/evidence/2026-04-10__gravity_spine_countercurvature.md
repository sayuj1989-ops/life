# Evidence Note: PIEZO1-GPX4 Axis and Ferroptosis in Scoliosis
Date: 2026-04-10

## Claim
Mechanical stress, specifically compressive loading, drives vertebral growth plate dysplasia (VGPD) and scoliosis progression by activating the mechanosensitive ion channel PIEZO1, which subsequently induces ferroptosis via the GPX4 signaling pathway.

## Mechanism
PIEZO1 upregulation in response to compressive stress disrupts GPX4 signaling. This disruption amplifies ferroptosis (iron-dependent programmed cell death) in vertebral growth plate chondrocytes, accelerating pathological ossification and growth plate degeneration. The pathway represents a "PIEZO1-GPX4-ferroptosis axis".

## Relevance to S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis
The vertebral growth plate is critical for axial elongation and symmetric growth. Asymmetric mechanical loading, which occurs inherently in an upright, S-shaped human spine under gravity, creates differential compressive stress. If this stress persistently activates the PIEZO1-GPX4 axis, it can lead to localized asymmetric growth plate dysplasia and premature ossification, driving the geometric drift seen in adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS). In animal models, conditional PIEZO1 knockout or pharmacological blockade decelerated scoliosis progression.

## Constraint/Support for the Counter-Curvature Hypothesis
**Supports**: The counter-curvature hypothesis frames scoliosis as an energy-minimization failure under gravitational load. The PIEZO1-ferroptosis axis provides a concrete, molecular "failure mode" where excessive compressive load (the bio-mechanical stress of upright posture) exceeds a cellular threshold, triggering cell death and structural degradation instead of adaptive remodeling. This perfectly aligns with the concept of a breakdown in the biological active force ($\mathbf{F}_{bio}$) needed to maintain the metastable S-shape.

## Open Question + Proposed Test
**Question:** Is the PIEZO1-GPX4-ferroptosis pathway active in early-stage, mechanically-induced curves before structural deformity is fixed, or is it purely a late-stage degenerative consequence?
**Test:** Induce mild asymmetrical compressive loading in a juvenile animal model (e.g., tail-tethered mouse) and assess vertebral growth plates for early ferroptosis markers (lipid peroxidation, GPX4 depletion) prior to the onset of fixed wedging.
