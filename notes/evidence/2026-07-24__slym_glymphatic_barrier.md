# Evidence Note: The SLYM Barrier and Glymphatic Stasis

**Date:** 2026-07-24
**Topic:** Glymphatics, SLYM, Microgravity, Inflammation
**Reference:** Møllgård, K., et al. "A mesothelium divides the subarachnoid space into functional compartments." *Science* 379.6627 (2023): 84-88. [mollgard2023slym]

## Claim
The **Subarachnoid Lymphatic-like Membrane (SLYM)**, identified by Møllgård et al. (2023), acts as a critical structural checkpoint for cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) flow and glymphatic exchange in the spine. We propose that this membrane functions as a **tension-dependent valve**: in microgravity, the cephalad fluid shift and loss of axial spinal tension cause the SLYM to slacken or distend, disrupting the organized separation of "clean" influx CSF and "dirty" efflux CSF. This failure drives the "Stagnant Pool" phenotype (`H_2026_06_23_Glymphatic_Stasis`).

## Mechanism
The SLYM divides the subarachnoid space into two distinct compartments, regulating the exchange of small molecules and preventing the free mixing of proteins.
1.  **Structural Gating**: As a mesothelium, the SLYM's permeability is likely regulated by mechanical tension (similar to fenestrated capillaries).
2.  **Microgravity Failure**: The "Fluid Shift" (puffy face, bird legs) increases intracranial and cervical venous pressure. This hydrostatic overload, combined with spinal elongation (unloading), may mechanically decouple the SLYM from its anchor points (trabeculae), causing it to collapse or lock into an impermeable state.
3.  **Toxic Accumulation**: Loss of SLYM function prevents the efficient clearance of metabolic waste (e.g., Amyloid-beta, Lactate) from the spinal parenchyma, creating a toxic "halo" around the nerve roots.

## Relevance to Counter-Curvature

Its failure explains why microgravity induces inflammatory responses (cytokines, MMP upregulation) in paraspinal tissues even in the absence of direct trauma. It links **fluid dynamics** to **structural degradation** (MMP-mediated softening), a key step in the scoliosis cascade.
