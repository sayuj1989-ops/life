# Evidence Note: Sleeping Posture as a Driver of Glymphatic Asymmetry and Scoliotic Curvature

**Date:** 2026-11-02
**Topic:** Glymphatic Clearance, Posture, and Asymmetric Inflammation
**References:**
- Lee, H., et al. (2015). "The Effect of Body Posture on Brain Glymphatic Transport." *Journal of Neuroscience*.
- Mader, J., et al. (2026). "Glymphatic stasis in the spinal cord during simulated microgravity." *Nature Neuroscience*.

## Claim
Chronic lateral sleeping preference induces asymmetric glymphatic clearance in the spinal cord due to gravitational pooling of interstitial fluid, leading to asymmetric accumulation of inflammatory mediators and scoliotic curvature.

## Mechanism
The glymphatic system relies on convective flow of CSF/ISF to clear metabolic waste (e.g., Lactate, Amyloid-beta). Lee et al. (2015) demonstrated that the lateral sleeping position optimizes global clearance compared to supine or prone positions. However, in the context of the spine, gravity creates a hydrostatic pressure gradient across the spinal cord diameter in the lateral position. This biases clearance flow, potentially causing stagnation of metabolic waste in the "dependent" (down-side) tissue relative to the "anti-dependent" (up-side) tissue.

## Relevance to Scoliosis / Counter-Curvature
If metabolic waste (including pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-6 and MMPs) accumulates asymmetrically, it drives asymmetric ECM remodeling. Specifically, MMP upregulation in the stagnant zone would lead to annular softening and collapse, initiating the scoliotic curve. This aligns with the "Energy Deficit" model where clearance failure acts as a metabolic "friction" preventing error correction. The "Counter-Curvature" hypothesis posits that gravity is required for proper orientation; here, *static* gravity during sleep creates a maladaptive bias if not alternated.

## Open Question
Does the "down" side in sleeping posture correspond to the concave or convex side of the scoliotic curve? Does the effect reverse in microgravity where convective forces are absent?

## Proposed Test
Correlate self-reported sleeping position (Right vs Left side) with the direction of the primary thoracic curve (Convexity) in a cohort of early-onset AIS patients. Use PET-MRI tracers to map spinal glymphatic flow in supine vs lateral positions.
