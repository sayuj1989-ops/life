# Evidence Note: The Tunable Antenna

## Claim
The primary cilium, long considered the cell's "antenna," is not a static structure but undergoes active length oscillation (approx. 24h period) driven by the core circadian clock (Bmal1/Clock). This oscillation tunes the cell's mechanosensitivity to the day/night cycle.

## Mechanism
Miyoshi et al. (2024) demonstrate that the centrosomal protein **Pericentrin** accumulates in centriolar satellites in a clock-dependent manner. This accumulation gates the availability of tubulin or transport machinery (IFT), causing cilia to elongate during one phase (e.g., rest/night) and shorten during another (e.g., active/day). The rhythm is abolished by Bmal1 knockout or the Rev-erb agonist SR9009.

## Relevance to S-Shaped Spinal Growth / Scoliosis
This finding provides a molecular mechanism for **"Spinal Jetlag"**:
1.  **Variable Sensitivity:** If cilia are longer at night (to sense subtle fluid flows during sleep/swelling) and shorter during the day (to avoid damage under high load), then the cell's "gain" is time-dependent.
2.  **Desynchrony:** In microgravity or under disrupted circadian cues (AIS patients with melatonin defects), this clock drifts. A "night-length" cilium exposed to "day-magnitude" forces (or vice versa) creates a sensory mismatch.
3.  **Repair Failure:** Fibroblast migration and ECM repair efficiency were shown to depend on this ciliary rhythm. Desynchrony halts repair, leading to the "accumulation of micro-damage" seen in scoliosis progression.

## Support for Counter-Curvature Hypothesis
The "Biological Counter-Curvature" theory posits that gravity is not just a load but a *signal* that entrains the spinal geometry. This paper confirms that the **sensor itself (the cilium) is entrained by the temporal structure of gravity (the day/night cycle)**.
- **Gravity as Zeitgeber:** If gravity entrains the clock (via Piezo1/YAP), and the clock tunes the cilium, then losing gravity breaks the loop: the antenna stops tuning, and the system goes "blind" or "deaf" to spatial cues.
