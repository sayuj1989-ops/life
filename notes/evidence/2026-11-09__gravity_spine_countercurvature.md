# Evidence Note: PIEZO1-GPX4 Ferroptosis Axis in Growth Plate Dysplasia

**Date:** 2026-11-09
**Topic:** Mechanotransduction, Gravity, Spine, Counter-Curvature

**Claim:** Mechanical compressive stress induces vertebral growth plate dysplasia by activating ferroptosis through the PIEZO1-GPX4 axis.

**Mechanism (Genes/Proteins/Pathways):**
Mechanical stress overactivates the mechanosensitive ion channel PIEZO1 in chondrocytes. This overactivation inhibits GPX4 signaling, leading to iron overload and ferroptosis (oxidative cell death), accelerating pathological ossification of the vertebral growth plate.

**Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis:**
This mechanism provides a direct cellular link between macroscopic mechanical loads (like gravity and asymmetric growth stress) and localized tissue failure (growth plate dysplasia) in the spine. When asymmetrical forces build up in the growing spine, this axis translates the resulting mechanical stress into cartilage degeneration and pathological ossification, actively driving the scoliotic curve.

**How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis:**
In the Biological Counter-Curvature framework, the spine must maintain an active metabolic standing wave to offset gravitational loading. If local stresses exceed the homeostatic capacity (the thermodynamic limit), PIEZO1 overactivation acts as a failure mode, triggering ferroptosis. This localized cellular death degrades the structural integrity ($EI$), locking in the scoliotic deformity and confirming that mechanical overload leads directly to localized tissue collapse.

**Open Question + Proposed Test:**
Does restoring GPX4 activity or inhibiting PIEZO1 mechanically decouple the growth plate from ferroptotic failure in microgravity-induced unloading?
*Test:* Subject wild-type and PIEZO1-knockout mice to simulated microgravity (hindlimb suspension) or hypergravity (centrifugation), measure ferroptosis markers (lipid ROS) and assess growth plate structure with or without GPX4 activators.
