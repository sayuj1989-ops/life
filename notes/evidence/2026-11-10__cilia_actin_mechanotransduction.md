# Evidence Note: Impaired Cilia-Actin Mechanotransduction in AIS Osteoblasts

**Date:** 2026-11-10
**Topic:** Mechanotransduction, Cilia, Spine, Counter-Curvature

**Claim:** Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) osteoblasts exhibit an inherent defect in mechanotransduction characterized by elongated primary cilia, altered cilia length dynamics under fluid flow, and compromised strain-induced actin filament rearrangement.

**Mechanism (Genes/Proteins/Pathways):**
Normally, applied bidirectional fluid flow induces osteoblasts to adjust primary cilia length and rearrange their actin cytoskeletal filaments to position and elongate in response to the mechanical load. In AIS osteoblasts, the primary cilia are intrinsically elongated and their length dynamics fail to respond correctly to fluid flow. Furthermore, strain-induced actin filament rearrangement is compromised. This defective mechanotransduction limits flow-induced cellular migration in AIS cells.

**Why it matters for S-shaped spinal growth / scoliosis:**
The growing adolescent spine undergoes constant mechanical stimulation for bone remodeling and elongation. If the primary sensory "antenna" (the primary cilium) and the structural effector (the actin cytoskeleton) in osteoblasts are fundamentally decoupled, the local bone tissue cannot appropriately adapt its geometry to macro-scale mechanical forces (like gravity or asymmetrical muscular tension). The resulting inability to position and elongate properly under load drives pathological differential growth.

**How it constrains or supports the counter-curvature hypothesis:**
The biological counter-curvature framework conceptualizes spinal stability as an active, energy-demanding metabolic process (Information-Elasticity Coupling) where sensory information is converted into local structural stiffness ($EI$) to oppose gravity. This finding strongly supports the "Information-Curvature Coupling" requirement. The system's ability to "read" the vector force (via primary cilia fluid flow dynamics) and translate it into a geometric correction (via actin remodeling) is fundamentally broken in AIS. Without this intact sensor-effector loop, the active gain required to maintain the straight counter-curvature against the $L^4$ scaling of buckling is lost.

**Open Question + Proposed Test:**
Does pharmacological stabilization or enhanced dynamic turnover of the actin cytoskeleton in AIS osteoblasts restore proper ciliary length adaptation and flow-induced migration?
*Test:* Culture AIS patient-derived osteoblasts and control osteoblasts in a parallel-plate flow chamber. Treat AIS cells with a transient dose of Jasplakinolide (actin stabilizer) or Latrunculin A (actin destabilizer) and quantify primary cilia length dynamics and migration velocity in response to oscillating fluid flow compared to vehicle-treated AIS cells.
