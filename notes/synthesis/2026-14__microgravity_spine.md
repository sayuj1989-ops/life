# Microgravity and Spine: Extracellular Matrix Remodeling

## Observed
1. Human mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs) exposed to simulated microgravity for 10 days show an imbalance in the collagen/non-collagen ratio, specifically a decrease in collagenous proteins (PMC8196606).
2. Simulated microgravity increases the secretion of proteases, including cathepsin A, cathepsin D, and MMP3, in MSCs (PMC8196606).
3. In microbes, decreased distortion in the cell surface due to lower fluid shear forces is an important mechanism for cellular mechanotransduction under microgravity, as observed in E. coli FimH sensor proteins (Frontiers in Pediatrics, 2016).

## Hypothesized
### Mechanistic Bridge
The loss of gravitational loading (unloading) reduces fluid shear stress across tissues. This reduction fundamentally alters the mechanotransduction environment, particularly the cell surface distortion required to activate mechanosensors. Without this mechanical signal, mesenchymal stem cells fail to maintain normal extracellular matrix (ECM) homeostasis. Instead, proteases (like MMP3 and cathepsins) are upregulated, leading to the degradation of collagenous proteins. This compromised ECM architecture dramatically reduces the structural stiffness ($EI$) of the spine, diminishing the active counter-curvature needed to resist buckling.

### Predicted Directionality
* **Unloading (Microgravity):** Decreased fluid shear stress $\rightarrow$ Reduced cell surface distortion / mechanosensor activation $\rightarrow$ Increased protease activity (MMP3, cathepsins) $\rightarrow$ Decreased collagen synthesis $\rightarrow$ ECM degradation $\rightarrow$ Loss of structural stiffness and potential scoliotic buckling.
* **Loading (1G Normal Gravity):** Optimal fluid shear stress $\rightarrow$ Normal mechanotransduction signaling $\rightarrow$ Balanced ECM turnover $\rightarrow$ Maintenance of active structural tone and proper S-shaped biological counter-curvature.
