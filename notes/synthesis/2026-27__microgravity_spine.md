## 1. Key Findings

### Observed: Spindle Silencing (Sensory Weighting)
Muscle spindles, the primary proprioceptors for muscle length and velocity, require a tonic level of background tension to maintain sensitivity. Proske & Gandevia (2012) demonstrate that in the absence of gravitational loading, the **gamma-motor drive** (which sets spindle sensitivity or "gain") is downregulated. This results in a "silent spindle" state where small deviations in spinal curvature fail to trigger the stretch reflex correction.

### Observed: Vestibular Mismatch & Sympathetic Loss
Gravity is the primary reference frame for the otolith organs. In microgravity, the conflict between otolith silence and visual/proprioceptive inputs creates "Sensory Conflict." Furthermore, Vignaux et al. (2013) showed that the vestibular system drives sympathetic outflow to bone; loss of this signal leads to rapid osteopenia. We extend this to the spine: loss of vestibular tone leads to loss of paraspinal muscle tone.

### Observed: Active Inference & Precision
Adams et al. (2013) frame motor control as **Active Inference**, where the brain minimizes "prediction error" (difference between expected and actual proprioception). Importantly, this process is weighted by **Precision** (reliability). In microgravity, the global "Gravity Vector" (high precision prior) is lost. The brain, facing high uncertainty, lowers the gain on proprioceptive errors to avoid "chasing noise," effectively allowing the spine to drift into maladaptive curvatures (scoliosis) without triggering corrective muscle activation.
