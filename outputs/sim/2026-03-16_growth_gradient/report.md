# Simulation Report: Growth Gradient Sweep
Date: 2026-03-16

## What changed
Swept the information field gradient from -2.0 to 2.0 (representing cranio-caudal differential growth) to observe its effect on emergent S-shapes and lateral buckling instability.

## What emergent shapes occurred
- Strong negative gradients (growth concentrated cranially) tended to produce moderate S-shapes but less pronounced scoliosis-like buckling (Cobb angles around 6-15 degrees).
- Intermediate gradients around 0 to 1.15 produced significant lateral instability, peaking at ~24-34 degrees Cobb, acting as an instability basin where symmetry breaks catastrophically.
- Extreme positive gradients (> 1.5) stabilized the spine back down to low Cobb angles (<4 degrees), potentially by shifting the apex away from vulnerable resonant regions.

## How this informs scoliosis vs normal S-curve
This suggests that the spatial distribution of growth (not just the magnitude) is critical for triggering lateral instability. A relatively uniform or slightly caudally biased growth pattern (gradient ~ 0.5 to 1.0) seems most dangerous, which aligns with adolescent growth spurts affecting the entire spine.
