# Phase 1, Day 1: Postural Ageing Literature Review

## Focus
Proprioceptive decline, vestibular degeneration, and cerebellar atrophy in ageing, and their relation to postural control.

## Key Findings

### 1. Proprioceptive Decline
Age-related proprioceptive decline is primarily attributed to reduced muscle spindle sensitivity, axonal atrophy, and neurochemical changes in the brain. These factors impair neuromuscular control and alter joint biomechanics, which reduces the speed and accuracy of movement planning and execution.
*Source: Proprioceptive Control of Muscle Activation in Aging: Implications for Balance and Fall Risk. Age Ageing. 2006. DOI: 10.1093/ageing/afl084 (Reference 7/Rubenstein L.Z. Falls in older people: Epidemiology, risk factors and strategies for prevention).*

Ageing affects the distal ankle strategy and the proximal hip strategy due to changes in trunk and leg proprioceptive functions.
*Source: Proprioception and Geriatric Low Back Pain. Aging. 1990;19(3):215-21.*

### 2. Cerebellar Atrophy
The cerebellum, responsible for integrating sensory inputs and predictive postural control, is one of the brain structures that ages the fastest, demonstrating significant neuronal loss and regional atrophy. Early cerebellar atrophy and abnormal activity closely mirror ageing-related functional decline and may precede clinical neurodegeneration.
*Source: Cerebellum and Aging: Update and Challenges. (ResearchGate, citing Gellersen et al., 2017; Tang et al., 2021; McElroy et al., 2024).*

## Implication for Paper 3
In adolescence (Paper 2), the "derivative gain gap" is a transient mismatch because the physical body changes faster than proprioception can adapt. In ageing (Paper 3), this gap becomes terminal. The physical degradation (sarcopenia, axonal atrophy, muscle spindle loss) and computational degradation (cerebellar neuronal loss) collectively act to permanently diminish the derivative gain ($K_d$) and increase the conduction delay ($\tau$), leading to irreversible postural instability and falls.
