# Phase 1, Day 2: Neuropathy and Delay in Ageing

## Objective
Search for papers on peripheral neuropathy and nerve conduction velocity (NCV) changes with age, quantifying how the overall proprioceptive transmission delay $\tau$ increases with age.

## Key Findings

### 1. Age-Related Decline in Nerve Conduction Velocity
- **Sensory NCV Decline**: Tong et al. (2004) and related studies estimate that sensory conduction velocity decreases at a rate of approximately 0.14 m/s per year of age [1, 2]. Other studies by Werner et al. suggest decreases up to 0.41 m/s per year [2].
- **Motor NCV Decline**: Falco et al. noted a 10% reduction in conduction rate by age 60, and several studies observe a significant decreasing trend starting as early as age 40 to 50 [2, 3].
- **Sural Nerve (Sensory) Decline**: Cross-sectional studies report that sensory NCV in the sural nerve drops from around 52.6 m/s in young individuals (20-30 years) to 40.1 m/s in older adults (60-80 years), representing roughly a 24% decrease over approximately 4-5 decades [4].
- **Underlying Mechanism**: The decline is attributed to physiological changes including progressive demyelination, axonal loss, decrease in nerve fiber density, and altered internodal lengths.

### 2. Quantification of Delay ($\tau$) Increase
- In a typical adult, normal sensory and motor NCV is around 50-60 m/s.
- For a 1-meter pathway (e.g., from foot proprioceptors to the spinal cord and back to leg muscles, or to the cerebellum), a baseline NCV of 60 m/s yields a pure nerve conduction delay of roughly $1 / 60 \approx 16.7$ ms (one-way) or $33.3$ ms (two-way).
- Note: The total postural delay $\tau$ (often measured around 150-200 ms) includes central processing and synaptic delays, which also increase with age.
- If NCV drops from ~55 m/s to ~40 m/s with age, the peripheral conduction portion of the delay increases proportionally: $1/40 \approx 25$ ms, adding roughly 16 ms to the two-way loop delay. While 16 ms seems small, when coupled with increased central processing delays, this degradation narrows the stable phase margin of the postural control system, predisposing it to the "terminal derivative gain gap."

### 3. Integration with Postural Control
- The decline in peripheral nerve conduction velocity directly impacts the integration between sensory and motor systems [5].
- When proprioceptive signals from the ankle and muscle spindles arrive later at the spinal cord and cerebellum, the derivative gain ($K_d$) must rely on outdated state information.
- Unlike the transient adolescent growth spurt (where the physical distance increases rapidly but nerves are healthy), the geriatric phase sees the conduction speed *itself* slowing down permanently due to irreversible neurodegeneration (e.g., Schwann cell senescence).
