# Phase 1, Day 2: Neuropathy and Nerve Conduction Velocity in Ageing

## Summary
The current body of literature demonstrates that peripheral nerve conduction velocity (NCV) universally decreases with advancing age. This degradation represents a core component of the "terminal derivative gain gap" thesis, as it directly increases the proprioceptive transmission delay ($\tau$) in the postural control loop.

## Key Findings

1. **Impact of Aging on Nerve Conduction Velocities and Late Responses in Healthy Individuals**
   * **Source:** Palve SS, Palve SB. J Neurosci Rural Pract. 2018 Jan-Mar;9(1):112-116. doi: 10.4103/jnrp.jnrp_323_17.
   * **Key points:**
     * Evaluated healthy individuals across three age groups (18-30, 31-45, 46-60).
     * Patients with older age had longer latencies, smaller amplitudes, and slower conduction velocities.
     * The study found that conduction velocity begins to decrease significantly as early as 40 years of age.
     * There was a significant negative correlation between age and both motor and sensory NCVs for median, peroneal, and tibial nerves.
     * The decline is potentially attributed to loss of myelinated and unmyelinated nerve fibers, reduction in nerve diameter, and decreased ATP production due to oxidative stress in mitochondria.

2. **Aging effects on conduction velocities of myelinated and unmyelinated fibers of peripheral nerves**
   * **Source:** Sato A, Sato Y, Suzuki H. Neurosci Lett. 1985 Jan 7;53(1):15-20. doi: 10.1016/0304-3940(85)90090-4.
   * **Key points:**
     * In an animal model (rats), the maximum conduction velocity of muscular gastrocnemius soleus (GS) myelinated fibers decreased by about 10-15% in late life stages.
     * Unmyelinated fiber conduction velocity remained relatively constant.
     * The study concludes that the majority of age-related changes in nerve conduction depend on changes in the myelin sheaths, rather than the unmyelinated components.

3. **General Consensus on Age-Related Change in Peripheral Nerve Conduction**
   * **Sources:** Multiple (Kurokawa & Mimori; general clinical guidelines).
   * **Key points:**
     * Age-related changes in compound muscle action potential (CMAP) amplitude, duration, and area occur uniformly in routine nerve conduction studies.
     * Decline in nerve conduction velocity and an increase in sensory latency are physiological hallmarks of normal aging, separate from pathological neuropathies.

## Relevance to the Terminal Derivative Gain Gap Model
* **Increase in $\tau$:** The progressive decline in NCV provides empirical justification for modeling $\tau$ (proprioceptive delay) as a monotonically increasing function of age in the geriatric PID model.
* **Myelin Sheath Implication:** The observation that age-related conduction slowing is primarily driven by myelin sheath alterations (Sato et al., 1985) perfectly sets up the telomere biology integration for Day 4. Specifically, if telomere shortening impairs Schwann cell function (which maintains peripheral myelin), this provides the molecular clock mechanism driving the age-related increase in $\tau$.
