# Phase 1, Day 5: LBX1 and Spinal Relay Delay

## Protein Overview
**LBX1 (Ladybird homeobox 1)** is a highly conserved transcription factor. It is broadly expressed in the developing dorsal spinal cord.

## Role in Dorsal Interneuron Specification
LBX1 is the critical developmental switch that determines the fate of several populations of dorsal interneurons (specifically dI4, dI5, and dI6). These interneurons form the first major relay station for sensory afferents entering the spinal cord.
*   **Proprioceptive Circuits:** Proprioceptive Group Ia/II afferents project heavily onto specific LBX1-dependent interneurons (like those in Clarke's column or their developmental equivalents) before ascending to the cerebellum.
*   **Relevance to $\tau_{spin}$:** The efficiency of synaptic transmission through this relay point dictates the **Spinal Relay Delay ($\tau_{spin}$)**. If an AIS-associated variant in the *LBX1* locus alters the expression gradient of the transcription factor during development, it could subtly mis-wire these interneuronal pools. A reduction in synaptic density, altered neurotransmitter receptor profiles, or less efficient dendritic arborization in these relay neurons would slow the signal integration time.

## Nav Channels in DRG Neurons
We additionally investigated voltage-gated sodium channels (e.g., Nav1.5 / Nav1.7 encoded by *SCN* genes) in the Dorsal Root Ganglion (DRG). The kinetics of these channels dictate action potential propagation speed. Slower inactivation kinetics or reduced channel density directly slow conduction velocity, combining with myelination defects (like those proposed for GPR126) to compound the **Afferent Conduction Delay ($\tau_{aff}$)**.

## Developmental Timing
Recent evidence suggests that the developmental timing of touch processing in the spinal cord is critical for establishing proper sensory-motor maps. We hypothesize that similar critical windows exist for proprioception, aligning closely with the pubertal growth spurt. An existing genetic vulnerability (e.g., LBX1 or GPR126) might be well-compensated during slow, steady growth in childhood, but fails to maintain sufficiently low $\tau$ values when the limb and spine geometry elongate rapidly.
