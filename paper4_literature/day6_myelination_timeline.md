# Day 6: Myelination Timeline During Adolescence

## Introduction
The pubertal growth spurt is a period of rapid skeletal elongation. Long bones in the extremities and the vertebral column grow at accelerated rates. This physical stretching of the body places unique biomechanical demands on the peripheral nervous system (PNS).

## The Biomechanics of Nerve Growth
As limbs elongate, the peripheral nerves that innervate them must also lengthen.
- **Axonal Stretch**: Axons can stretch in response to mechanical tension, a process sometimes called "stretch growth."
- **Myelin Remodeling**: Schwann cells do not simply stretch infinitely. As the internodal distance (the length of myelin between two Nodes of Ranvier) increases due to bone growth, the Schwann cell must synthesize massive amounts of new myelin membrane to maintain the optimal $g$-ratio (the ratio of inner axonal diameter to total outer diameter including myelin).
- **The Lag Phase**: Research into peripheral nerve development (e.g., Schlaepfer & Myers, 1973, DOI: 10.1002/cne.901470207) indicates that internode elongation correlates with growth. However, if bone growth velocity exceeds the metabolic capacity of Schwann cells to synthesize new myelin, a temporary "thinning" of the myelin sheath occurs.

## Relevance to $\tau$ and AIS
This transient state of suboptimal myelination means that the capacitance of the internodal membrane increases, slowing the saltatory conduction of action potentials.
Therefore, during peak height velocity (PHV) in puberty, there is an expected, physiological increase in $\tau_{afferent}$ and $\tau_{efferent}$.
In healthy adolescents, this minor delay is tolerated by the postural control system. However, in individuals with genetic variants that impair myelination efficiency (e.g., GPR126 hypomorphs), this physiological lag becomes severely exacerbated, pushing total $\tau$ past the critical threshold.
