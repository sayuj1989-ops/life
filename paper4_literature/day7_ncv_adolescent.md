# Day 7: Nerve Conduction Velocity Changes During Adolescence

## Introduction
To formalize the components of $\tau$, we need quantitative data on how nerve conduction velocity (NCV) changes with age, height, and during the adolescent growth spurt.

## NCV and Height
It is a well-established neurophysiological principle that nerve conduction velocity varies inversely with height (e.g., Campbell et al., 1981, DOI: 10.1002/mus.880040609; Halonen & Lang, 1983, DOI: 10.1016/0013-4694(83)92418-5).
- Taller individuals have slightly slower NCVs in their lower extremities.
- This is thought to be due to distal axonal tapering (axons become thinner further from the cell body) and cooler temperatures in distal limbs.

## The Conduction Delay Calculation
The afferent delay ($\tau_{afferent}$) is simply the distance the signal must travel divided by the conduction velocity:
$\tau_{afferent} = rac{	ext{Path Length}}{	ext{NCV}}$

During the adolescent growth spurt, two things happen simultaneously:
1. **Path Length Increases**: The spine and legs lengthen rapidly.
2. **NCV May Transiently Drop or Plateau**: Due to the myelin remodeling lag (discussed in Day 6) and the inverse relationship with height, NCV does not increase fast enough to offset the longer path length.

**Result**: $\tau_{afferent}$ (and $\tau_{efferent}$) strictly increases during the adolescent growth spurt.

## Synthesis for the Model
If baseline NCV is $\approx 60$ m/s, and a child grows from 1.2m to 1.6m, the path length for a postural reflex (e.g., from soleus to spinal cord) increases.
$\tau_{afferent}$ might increase from $\approx 10$ ms to $\approx 15$ ms.
If an individual carries the GPR126 risk allele, their NCV might be $50$ m/s instead of $60$ m/s, pushing their $\tau_{afferent}$ from $15$ ms to $18$ ms. When combined with other polygenic hits (PIEZO2, LBX1), this small absolute increase is the difference between stable posture and buckling instability.
