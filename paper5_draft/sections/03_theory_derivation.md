# 3. Theory: The Spine as a Predictive Processor

In this section, we formally derive the delayed PID controller of Paper 2 as the optimal controller under a specific linear Gaussian generative model within the active inference framework. We build upon the foundation established by Baltieri & Buckley (2019), extending their formalism to include the two defining characteristics of the adolescent spine: non-stationary plant parameters (growth) and delayed sensory observations.

## 3.1 The Generative Model of Postural Control

We define a linear Gaussian generative model $p(\tilde{y}, \tilde{x}, v, \theta)$ for the postural control system.

Let the hidden states (e.g., the trunk angle relative to vertical) be represented in continuous time using generalised coordinates of motion: $\tilde{x} = [x, x', x'', \dots]^T$, where $x$ is position, $x'$ is velocity, and so forth.

The generative model specifies the joint probability of sensory observations $\tilde{y}$ and hidden states $\tilde{x}$, parameterised by hidden causes $v$ (e.g., persistent gravitational torque) and the model's estimate of the plant's physical parameters $\theta_{model}$ (e.g., mass, length).

$$ p(\tilde{y}, \tilde{x}, v) = p(\tilde{y} | \tilde{x}) p(\tilde{x} | v) p(v) $$

### 3.1.1 The Delayed Sensory Likelihood

A critical feature of the human postural control system is the temporal transmission delay, $\tau$, between the true state of the body and the arrival of proprioceptive, visual, and vestibular sensory information at the central nervous system.

The observations $y(t)$ are a delayed mapping of the true states $x(t - \tau)$ plus sensory noise $\omega_y$:

$$ y(t) = g(x(t - \tau)) + \omega_y $$

Assuming a linear observation mapping $g(x) = Cx$, we can express this in generalised coordinates:

$$ \tilde{y} = C \tilde{x}_\tau + \tilde{\omega}_y $$

where $\tilde{x}_\tau$ represents the delayed state. The sensory noise $\tilde{\omega}_y$ is drawn from a multivariate Gaussian distribution with zero mean and a precision matrix $\Pi_y$ (the inverse of the covariance matrix, $\Sigma_y^{-1}$).

### 3.1.2 The State Dynamics (Priors)

The state evolves continuously according to a linear function $f$, driven by the hidden causes $v$ and subject to state noise $\omega_x$:

$$ \tilde{x}' = f(\tilde{x}, v) + \tilde{\omega}_x $$

Which we can express linearly as:

$$ \tilde{x}' = A \tilde{x} + B v + \tilde{\omega}_x $$

The state noise $\tilde{\omega}_x$ is assumed to be Gaussian with precision matrix $\Pi_x$.

## 3.2 Variational Free Energy Minimisation

The brain seeks to minimise variational free energy $F$, which bounds the surprise of its sensory observations. Under the Laplace assumption (which approximates the posterior probability as a Gaussian density centered on its mode, $\tilde{\mu}$), $F$ becomes a simple function of the precision-weighted prediction errors $\tilde{\varepsilon}$:

$$ F \approx \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\varepsilon}_y^T \Pi_y \tilde{\varepsilon}_y + \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\varepsilon}_x^T \Pi_x \tilde{\varepsilon}_x $$

where the prediction errors are defined as the difference between the true signals and the brain's internal expectations (the mode of the posterior, $\mu$):

1.  **Sensory Prediction Error ($\tilde{\varepsilon}_y$):** The difference between the actual delayed sensory input $\tilde{y}$ and the predicted delayed sensory input based on the model's state estimate $\tilde{\mu}_{x,\tau}$:
    $$ \tilde{\varepsilon}_y = \tilde{y} - C \tilde{\mu}_{x,\tau} $$

2.  **State/Dynamics Prediction Error ($\tilde{\varepsilon}_x$):** The difference between the estimated temporal evolution of the state ($D\tilde{\mu}_x$, where $D$ is the derivative operator) and the predicted evolution based on the generative model's dynamics $f$:
    $$ \tilde{\varepsilon}_x = D\tilde{\mu}_x - f(\tilde{\mu}_x, v) $$

## 3.3 Active Inference Reduces to Delayed PID Control

In active inference, action $a$ (e.g., muscle torque generated by spinal reflex arcs) acts to minimise free energy by changing the sensory input $\tilde{y}$ to match the predictions, rather than changing the internal model. The system descends the free energy gradient with respect to action:

$$ \dot{a} = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial a} = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial \tilde{y}} \frac{\partial \tilde{y}}{\partial a} $$

If we expand this gradient descent for the first two orders of motion (position $y$ and velocity $y'$), and assume the top-down desired state (the set-point) is the upright posture $r=0$, the resulting control law is:

$$ a(t) \propto \Pi_{y,0} (y(t) - r) + \Pi_{y,1} (y'(t) - r') + \Pi_v \int (y(\sigma) - r) d\sigma $$

This equation is structurally identical to the delayed PID control law defined in Section 2.2. Crucially, we can formally map the components:

*   **Proportional Gain ($K_p$) $\leftrightarrow \Pi_{y,0}$**: The precision on the position prediction error. A high $\Pi_{y,0}$ means the system strongly penalises positional deviations from the set-point.
*   **Derivative Gain ($K_d$) $\leftrightarrow \Pi_{y,1}$**: The precision on the velocity prediction error. A high $\Pi_{y,1}$ means the system strongly penalises rapid changes (velocity), providing necessary damping.
*   **Integral Gain ($K_i$) $\leftrightarrow \Pi_v$**: The precision on the slowly varying prior/bias $v$, correcting for steady-state errors like a persistent gravitational load.

This derivation confirms the foundation of our bridge: The classical PID controller is not just an analogy; it is the mathematically optimal solution for free energy minimisation under a linear generative model. The $K_d$ gain *is* the precision weighting of velocity prediction errors.

## 3.4 The Derivative Gain Gap as Precision Collapse

We now apply this active inference framework to the non-stationary, growing plant.

In Paper 2, we modelled the adolescent growth spurt as a rapid change in the plant's parameters (mass $m(t)$ and length $L(t)$) at a velocity $v_{growth} = \dot{\theta}_{plant}$. The brain's generative model relies on an internal estimate of these parameters, $\theta_{model}$.

The true biomechanical dynamics of the spine depend on $\theta_{plant}$:

$$ x_{true}'' = f_{biomech}(x, x', \theta_{plant}) $$

The generative model predicts these dynamics using $\theta_{model}$:

$$ \mu_x'' = f_{model}(\mu_x, \mu_x', \theta_{model}) $$

During a rapid adolescent growth spurt, the rate of change of the body ($v_{growth}$) exceeds the Bayesian learning rate of the model. The model's parameters fall behind reality: $\theta_{model}(t) \neq \theta_{plant}(t)$.

This model misspecification means the generative model's predictions about the acceleration ($x''$) and velocity ($x'$) of the spine are systematically biased. Consequently, the empirical variance ($\sigma_{y'}^2$) of the sensory prediction errors on velocity ($\varepsilon_{y'} = y' - \mu_y'$) increases persistently over time.

In hierarchical predictive processing, precisions $\Pi$ are not fixed constants; they are hyperparameters that are themselves dynamically optimised to minimise free energy. If the variance of a prediction error signal increases, the optimal Bayesian inference is to down-weight its precision (since precision is the inverse of variance, $\Pi = \sigma^{-2}$).

The optimal precision $\Pi_{y'}^*$ is given by:

$$ \Pi_{y'}^* = \frac{1}{\sigma_{y'}^2 + \lambda} $$

where $\lambda$ is a baseline noise term.

Since the variance of the velocity error $\sigma_{y'}^2$ scales with the degree of model misspecification, which in turn scales with the product of growth velocity $v_{growth}$ and the proprioceptive delay $\tau$ (because the error accumulates over the delay period before it can be corrected):

$$ \sigma_{y'}^2 \approx k(v_{growth} \cdot \tau) $$

Therefore, the precision on velocity prediction errors collapses according to:

$$ \Pi_{y'}^* \propto \frac{1}{v_{growth} \cdot \tau + \lambda} $$

Because we have established that $K_d \leftrightarrow \Pi_{y'}$, this provides the formal derivation for the central mechanism of Paper 2:

$$ K_{d, effective} \propto \frac{1}{v_{growth} \cdot \tau + \lambda} $$

The transient degradation of derivative gain ($K_d$) during adolescence is mathematically equivalent to the optimal Bayesian down-weighting of sensory precision on velocity prediction errors. The brain correctly detects that its velocity predictions are systematically corrupted by the rapidly changing body, and it optimally chooses to ignore them to avoid acting on "noise." This is a "healthy" cognitive response that unfortunately triggers a catastrophic biomechanical failure.
