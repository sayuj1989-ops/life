# 5. Discussion

The reframing of adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) as a free-energy catastrophe provides a powerful new perspective that bridges the gap between biomechanical orthopaedics and computational neuroscience. This synthesis not only validates the "derivative gain gap" model of Paper 2 from first principles but also generates significant clinical and theoretical implications.

## 5.1 Clinical Implications: Modulating Precision

Current conservative treatments for AIS, primarily rigid bracing and physiotherapeutic scoliosis-specific exercises (PSSE) like the Schroth method, are largely empirical. Bracing provides passive mechanical support, while PSSE aims to strengthen specific muscle groups and improve postural awareness.

The active inference framework offers a precise mechanism for *why* these interventions work, and suggests novel avenues for treatment based on the modulation of sensory precision ($\Pi$).

In the FEP framework, the derivative gain gap is a transient collapse in the precision of proprioceptive velocity prediction errors ($\Pi_{vel} \downarrow$). Because the brain does not trust its sensory feedback regarding spinal movement, it fails to generate the necessary high-precision corrective reflex actions (the "effective $K_d$" drops).

### 5.1.1 Attention as a Precision Modulator

A key tenet of predictive processing is that **attention** is the mechanism by which the brain dynamically optimises precision. Directing top-down attention to a specific sensory channel transiently increases its precision weighting.

If the core deficit in AIS is a down-weighting of proprioceptive precision, then interventions that explicitly train interoceptive and proprioceptive attention should partially compensate for the gain gap. This explains the efficacy of PSSE methods that emphasise intense "postural awareness" rather than just brute-force muscle strengthening. By forcing the patient to attend to the subtle positional and velocity cues of their spine, they are actively up-weighting $\Pi_{vel}$, temporarily restoring the effective damping ($K_d$) required for stability.

### 5.1.2 The Protective Effect of Interoceptive Practices

Extending this logic, practices that systematically train the maintenance of high interoceptive precision—such as specific forms of mindfulness, body-scanning meditation, or proprioceptive-focused disciplines (e.g., yoga, Tai Chi, Pilates)—could serve a prophylactic role during the critical peak height velocity (PHV) window.

If an adolescent can maintain a high baseline precision weighting on their spinal proprioception through dedicated training, they may be better equipped to withstand the "noise" induced by the rapid morphological changes of the growth spurt. This suggests a shift in focus from purely mechanical correction to neuro-cognitive training for postural control.

### 5.1.3 Bracing as a "Sensory Anchor"

Rigid bracing is traditionally viewed purely as a mechanical constraint that forces the spine into alignment. However, from an active inference perspective, a brace also functions as a powerful, high-precision sensory anchor.

The brace provides continuous, unambiguous tactile and proprioceptive feedback about the position of the trunk. By providing a reliable sensory signal that the generative model can trust, the brace artificially boosts the precision of the sensory input ($\Pi_{pos}$). This reduces the overall free energy of the upright posture and helps the system escape the pathological local minimum (the scoliotic curve).

The active inference framework thus unifies the mechanical and neurological aspects of AIS treatment: successful intervention must either physically constrain the plant (bracing) or neurologically restore the precision of the generative model (attentional training).
