## Optimal Control vs. Active Inference
Traditional motor control theories rely on optimal control frameworks:
1. **Inverse Models:** The brain computes the motor commands required to achieve a desired trajectory.
2. **Forward Models:** The brain predicts the sensory consequences of those motor commands (corollary discharge) to compensate for sensory delays and attenuate self-generated sensations.

**Active Inference** replaces this dichotomy:
- The brain *only* uses forward models (generative models).
- There are no explicit "motor commands" representing forces or torques computed in the cortex.
- Instead, the cortex generates *predictions* of the proprioceptive trajectories it expects to experience.

## The Reflex Arc as Free-Energy Minimization
In active inference, the organism minimizes free energy (prediction error) through two pathways:
1. **Perception:** Updating internal beliefs to match sensory input (minimizing exteroceptive prediction errors).
2. **Action:** Changing the state of the world to match predictions.

Motor control is pure action. The cortex sends strong, descending predictions of proprioceptive states (e.g., "my spine is straight") to the alpha motor neurons in the spinal cord.
- The mismatch between the current proprioceptive state (e.g., "my spine is curved") and the descending prediction creates a proprioceptive prediction error.
- The classical reflex arc (stretch reflex) simply resolves this error by contracting the relevant muscles until the sensory feedback matches the prediction.
- Thus, movement is the process of fulfilling a self-fulfilling prophecy.

## The Equilibrium-Point Hypothesis
Active inference naturally subsumes the Equilibrium-Point Hypothesis (EPH) of motor control.
- In EPH, the brain does not specify muscle forces but rather shifts the equilibrium point (threshold) of the stretch reflex.
- Under active inference, shifting the equilibrium point is identical to changing the descending proprioceptive prediction. The spinal cord automatically generates the necessary restorative forces to reach the new predicted state.

## Relevance to Paper 5: The Derivative Gain Gap
This framework provides a direct mapping to the Paper 2 model:
- The setpoint of the inverted pendulum is the descending proprioceptive prediction ("upright posture").
- The restoring torques generated by the PID controller are exactly the reflex arc fulfilling this prediction.
- Crucially, this process depends on **precision weighting**. The system must confidently "believe" its descending predictions while simultaneously down-weighting ascending sensory errors, otherwise, it would simply update its belief to match the current (curved) posture rather than moving to correct it.
- During rapid growth, the generative model's mapping between predictions and actual kinematics is degraded (the derivative component). If precision collapses on these proprioceptive predictions, the system loses the ability to drive the reflex arc effectively. Instead of moving to minimize error, it might simply update its internal model to accept the abnormal posture, forming a local free-energy minimum (scoliosis).
