# Day 7: Bayesian Models of Sensorimotor Development

## Core Concepts
Development is characterized by continuous updating of the brain's internal models of the body. From a Bayesian perspective, this is the continuous recalibration of priors and likelihoods based on sensory evidence.
During periods of stable, slow growth, the generative model's learning rate can easily track the physical changes in the body's parameters (mass, length, moments of inertia). The "prediction errors" generated by movement are small and easily integrated to update the model.

## The Problem of the Growth Spurt
The adolescent growth spurt (particularly the peak height velocity, which is central to the Paper 2 model) represents a unique challenge.
The rate of change of the "plant" ($v_{growth}$) exceeds the rate at which the neural model can update its priors ($\tau_{learning}$).
In Bayesian terms:
*   The likelihood function (the distribution of expected sensory feedback given a motor command) becomes systematically misaligned with reality.
*   Because the predictions about the body's dynamics (velocity, acceleration) are consistently wrong, the variance of the prediction error increases.
*   Optimal Bayesian inference dictates that when the variance of a signal increases, its precision (inverse variance, $\Pi$) must be down-weighted.

The brain correctly infers: "My predictions about how fast my limbs/trunk will move are currently unreliable. I should not trust them."
This adaptive down-weighting of sensory precision on velocity prediction errors is the mathematical equivalent of the "derivative gain gap" ($K_d$ degradation).

## Sensory Precision and Development
Studies on sensorimotor development show that children and adolescents often exhibit transient periods of clumsiness or altered multisensory integration during rapid growth phases. This is consistent with a temporary re-weighting of sensory precision as internal models are updated.
