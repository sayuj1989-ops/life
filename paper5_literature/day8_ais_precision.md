## Existing Evidence
There is a substantial body of evidence that patients with Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) exhibit altered sensory processing and postural control:
*   **Atypical Multisensory Integration:** AIS patients often show difficulty integrating visual, vestibular, and proprioceptive information. When one sensory modality is removed or perturbed (e.g., eyes closed, standing on a foam pad), their postural sway increases more than in healthy controls.
*   **Body Representation Deficits:** Studies suggest AIS patients have an altered internal representation of their body schema, particularly their trunk position and orientation in space.
*   **Proprioceptive Reweighting:** The nervous system dynamically reweights the importance of different sensory inputs based on their reliability. In AIS, there's evidence that proprioceptive signals from the spine and lower limbs are less reliable, leading to an over-reliance on visual and vestibular cues for balance.

## The FEP Connection
The FEP framework provides a unified explanation for these observations:
1.  **Precision Collapse:** The initial trigger is the rapid growth spurt, which renders the brain's internal model of the spine's dynamics (velocity, acceleration) inaccurate.
2.  **Down-weighting Proprioception:** Because the predictions about proprioceptive feedback are consistently wrong, the brain optimally down-weights the precision (reliability) of these signals. This manifests as the "derivative gain gap" in postural control.
3.  **Compensatory Reweighting:** To maintain balance with less reliable proprioception, the brain increases the precision weighting of visual and vestibular inputs. This explains the increased sway when these compensatory systems are challenged.
4.  **Altered Body Schema:** The persistent mismatch between predicted and actual sensory feedback, coupled with the down-weighting of proprioception, leads to a degraded internal representation of the body's state (the posterior distribution becomes wider and less certain).

This aligns perfectly with the predictive processing view: AIS is not just a biomechanical failure, but a failure of the brain's generative model to maintain a precise and accurate representation of the growing body.
