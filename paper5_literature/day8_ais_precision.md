# Phase 2, Day 8: Proprioceptive precision in scoliosis

## Existing Evidence: Altered Sensory Weighting in AIS
The literature on Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) already contains significant clues pointing toward a sensory reweighting deficit, which strongly supports our precision-collapse hypothesis.

- **Simoneau et al. (2006):** Demonstrated that AIS patients rely less on proprioceptive inputs and more on visual inputs for postural control compared to healthy controls. In FEP terms, this is a clear shift in precision weighting ($\Pi_{proprioception} \downarrow$, $\Pi_{vision} \uparrow$).
- **Haumont et al. (2011):** Found evidence of altered sensory integration and a persistent reweighting of sensory inputs in AIS patients. They suggested a central integration deficit rather than a peripheral nerve issue.
- **Blecher et al. (2018):** Showed that AIS patients have altered cortical processing of proprioceptive signals (using EEG/SSEP), supporting the idea that the "error" is in the central generative model's handling of the signals, not the signals themselves.
- **Body Schema Deficits:** Several studies indicate that AIS patients have an altered internal representation of verticality (Subjective Postural Vertical) and body schema. Their internal model's "prior" for straightness may be miscalibrated or highly uncertain (low precision).

## Linking Evidence to the PP/FEP Framework
1. **The Core Deficit:** The primary deficit in AIS is not a hardware failure (like a severed nerve), but a *software* failure in precision allocation. The brain learns to distrust its proprioceptive signals during the rapid growth phase because they systematically violate its outdated predictions.
2. **Visual Compensation:** Because $\Pi_{proprioception}$ drops, the system attempts to minimize free energy by relying on other sensory channels, like vision. However, visual feedback is too slow (longer delay $\tau$) to adequately compensate for the loss of fast, derivative-like proprioceptive feedback ($K_d$), leaving the system vulnerable to the "Derivative Gain Gap" instability.
3. **The Local Minimum:** When the system cannot maintain the unstable upright posture, it settles into a new, stable mechanical equilibrium (scoliosis). Over time, the generative model *updates its priors* to expect this new curved posture. The scoliosis becomes the new high-precision prior, making it difficult to correct.

## Testable Clinical Predictions
- **Prediction 1:** AIS patients should show a measurable drop in proprioceptive precision specifically during peak height velocity.
- **Prediction 2:** Interventions that force attention to proprioception (top-down precision enhancement) should temporarily improve postural stability in AIS.
