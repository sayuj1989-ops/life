# Mathematical Bridge: PID Control to Active Inference

This document establishes the formal mathematical mapping between the delayed PID inverted pendulum model of postural control (from Paper 3) and the Active Inference framework using generalized coordinates of motion (Paper 5).

## 1. Classical PID Postural Control (Paper 3)

In Paper 3, postural control is modeled as an inverted pendulum governed by a delayed PID controller. The corrective torque $\tau_c$ applied at the base is:

$$ \tau_c(t) = -K_p \theta(t - \Delta t) - K_d \dot{\theta}(t - \Delta t) - K_i \int \theta(t - \Delta t) dt $$

Where:
- $\theta$ is the angular deviation from vertical.
- $K_p$, $K_d$, and $K_i$ are the proportional, derivative, and integral gains, respectively.
- $\Delta t$ is the sensory-motor delay.

The central hypothesis of Paper 3 is the "Derivative Gain Gap": during rapid adolescent growth, the biomechanical changes outpace sensory adaptation, leading to a functional degradation of the derivative gain ($K_d \rightarrow 0$).

## 2. Active Inference Formulation (Paper 5)

Active inference proposes that the brain minimizes variational free energy ($F$), which acts as an upper bound on sensory surprise. Action ($a$) is generated to fulfill descending predictions by minimizing sensory prediction errors via peripheral reflex arcs.

$$ a = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial a} $$

### Generalized Coordinates of Motion

Sensory states ($s$) and internal beliefs ($\mu$) are represented in generalized coordinates of motion:
- $\tilde{s} = [s, s', s'', \dots]^T$ (position, velocity, acceleration, etc.)
- $\tilde{\mu} = [\mu, \mu', \mu'', \dots]^T$

### Prediction Errors

Prediction errors ($\tilde{\varepsilon}$) are the difference between actual sensory input and the predicted sensory input generated by the internal model ($g(\tilde{\mu})$):

$$ \tilde{\varepsilon} = \tilde{s} - g(\tilde{\mu}) $$

Expanding this for the first two orders (position and velocity):
- $\varepsilon_0 = s - g(\mu)$ (Position Error)
- $\varepsilon_1 = s' - g'(\mu, \mu')$ (Velocity Error)

### Precision Weighting

These errors are weighted by a precision matrix ($\Pi$), which encodes the inverse variance (reliability) of the sensory channels:

$$ F \approx \frac{1}{2} \tilde{\varepsilon}^T \Pi \tilde{\varepsilon} $$

Assuming a block-diagonal precision matrix for simplicity:
$$ F \approx \frac{1}{2} \left( \Pi_0 \varepsilon_0^2 + \Pi_1 \varepsilon_1^2 \right) $$

### Action Generation (Gradient Descent)

Action minimizes free energy by changing sensory inputs ($s$ and $s'$):

$$ \dot{a} = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial a} = -\left( \frac{\partial \varepsilon_0}{\partial a} \Pi_0 \varepsilon_0 + \frac{\partial \varepsilon_1}{\partial a} \Pi_1 \varepsilon_1 \right) $$

Using the chain rule, $\frac{\partial \varepsilon_i}{\partial a} = \frac{\partial s^{(i)}}{\partial a}$. This partial derivative represents the mapping from action to sensory consequences (the reflex arc).

Assuming a simple linear mapping where action directly affects force/acceleration, integrating this to find the equivalent force yields a control law of the form:

$$ Action \propto - \Pi_0 \varepsilon_0 - \Pi_1 \varepsilon_1 $$

## 3. The Bridge

By comparing the Active Inference action update with the classical PID equation, we establish the following direct equivalences:

1.  **Proportional Gain ($K_p$) $\equiv$ Precision of Position Errors ($\Pi_0$)**: The system's responsiveness to positional deviations maps to how reliable it believes the positional sensory channels are.
2.  **Derivative Gain ($K_d$) $\equiv$ Precision of Velocity Errors ($\Pi_1$)**: The system's responsiveness to the rate of change maps directly to the precision weighting of velocity prediction errors.
3.  **Position ($\theta$) $\equiv$ Position Error ($\varepsilon_0$)**: Assuming the descending prediction $g(\mu)$ specifies an upright equilibrium state ($g(\mu) = 0$), then $\varepsilon_0 = \theta$.
4.  **Velocity ($\dot{\theta}$) $\equiv$ Velocity Error ($\varepsilon_1$)**: Similarly, assuming the target velocity is zero, $\varepsilon_1 = \dot{\theta}$.

## 4. Re-interpreting the "Derivative Gain Gap"

With this mathematical bridge, the central hypothesis of adolescent scoliosis onset is reframed under the Free Energy Principle:

**Biomechanical Hypothesis (Paper 3):** Rapid skeletal growth increases the inertia and natural frequency of the spine, rendering the existing derivative gain ($K_d$) insufficient to prevent oscillatory instability (wobble).

**Active Inference Reframing (Paper 5):**
1. Rapid bone growth alters the true mapping between motor commands and sensory consequences (the generative model is suddenly outdated).
2. Because the limbs are longer and heavier, movements produce unpredicted velocities, leading to a persistent increase in velocity prediction errors ($\varepsilon_1$).
3. To minimize free energy ($F$), the system has two choices: update the generative model (slow, requires learning) or down-weight the precision of the 'noisy' signal.
4. The brain optimally down-weights the precision of velocity errors ($\Pi_1 \downarrow$) to minimize $F$ in the short term.
5. Because $\Pi_1 \equiv K_d$, this precision down-weighting *is exactly* the degradation of the derivative gain, causing the delayed PID controller to cross the bifurcation boundary into instability.

This bridges the gap between biomechanical morphology (growth) and computational neurobiology (precision weighting in predictive processing).
