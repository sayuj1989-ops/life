## Hypothesized Mechanical Role
**"Mechanotransduction Block-Spring Hubs" / Dystrophin-Glycoprotein Complex Anchoring**
The proteins in this cluster exhibit moderate-low anisotropy (1.92) but extremely high PAE blockiness (6.53). This structural proxy signifies a "beads-on-a-string" architecture consisting of rigid globular domains separated by highly flexible, disordered linker regions.
This geometry is perfectly suited for "Tension-Gated Signal Integrators." The rigid blocks serve as binding hubs for signaling molecules (like IGF1R, NTRK3 kinases) or structural anchors (DMD, DAG1 to the extracellular matrix/sarcolemma), while the flexible linkers act as mechanical springs. Under resting conditions, the domains remain clustered (high effective blockiness). When subjected to gravitational or mechanical loading, the flexible linkers extend, pulling the rigid blocks apart. This tension-dependent unfolding exposes cryptic binding sites or mechanically activates kinase domains (e.g., MYLK).
In the context of the spine, this cluster likely forms the primary mechanical anchor between the paraspinal muscle cytoskeleton and the extracellular matrix (via the Dystrophin-Glycoprotein complex: DMD/DAG1).

## Concrete Test
**Hypothesis:** Mechanical unloading (simulated microgravity) will cause "hyper-blockiness" (coiling) of DMD/DAG1 complexes, preventing tension-gated activation of associated signaling kinases (IGF1R/NTRK3) and leading to scoliotic muscle imbalance.
**Test:** Culture patient-derived paraspinal myoblasts under static (1G) versus clinorotation (simulated microgravity). Use FRET-based tension sensors embedded in DMD or DAG1 to measure the in vivo "blockiness" (coiling vs extension). Concurrently, measure the phosphorylation states of downstream targets of IGF1R and NTRK3. The prediction is high FRET (coiled/hyper-blocky state) and low kinase activity in microgravity compared to 1G controls.
