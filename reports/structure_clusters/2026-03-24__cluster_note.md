## Shared Properties
The proteins in this cluster share a distinct "strain gauge" structural profile:
- **High Anisotropy (Mean: 3.93):** An elongated, rigid structure that aligns with stress vectors.
- **Low Intrinsic Disorder (Mean: 0.13):** A lack of flexible domains, meaning these proteins act more like stiff rods than flexible ropes.

## Hypothesized Mechanical Role
**Rigid-Body Integrators / Vectorial Mechanosensors:**
Because these proteins are highly anisotropic and lack flexible disordered regions, they act as "rigid-body integrators." They filter out high-frequency thermal and local fluctuations, allowing them to detect weak but consistent global signals like the continuous pull of gravity or sustained tension. Rather than responding to scalar compression, they act as vectorial sensors requiring tension alignment. In the context of the biological counter-curvature hypothesis, they provide the noise-filtered reference signal for spinal alignment. Loss of this rigidity or misalignment of the sensor (e.g., in microgravity or through mutation) would result in a loss of the directional reference signal, leading to sensory drift and scoliotic buckling.

## Falsifiable Test
**Experiment:** Engineer ACVR1 or PIEZO2 variants with disordered, flexible linkers inserted into their rigid domains.
**Prediction:** Replacing the rigid domains with flexible linkers will increase signaling noise and disrupt the detection of consistent global tension.
**Measurement:** Measure signaling variance (noise) versus the mean signal in cells expressing the engineered variants under static load compared to wild-type cells. We predict an increase in signaling variance and a failure to properly orient or respond to the static load.
