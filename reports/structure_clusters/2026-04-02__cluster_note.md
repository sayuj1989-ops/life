## Shared Properties
The proteins in this cluster are characterized by high anisotropy (mean ~3.93) and low intrinsic disorder (mean ~0.13). This "rigid rod" or "extended scaffold" geometry is particularly suited for directional mechanosensing, as the high aspect ratio and structural rigidity allow the proteins to efficiently transmit vector forces (e.g., tension, shear) across large spatial scales without dampening the signal through internal flexibility.

## Hypothesized Mechanical Role
**Rigid-Body Vector Sensing / Tension-Bearing Scaffolds**
Given their specific geometrical properties, PIEZO2 and ACVR1 likely act as macro-scale tension sensors within the extracellular matrix or transmembrane space. Unlike flexible domains that absorb mechanical energy, these rigid extended structures act like mechanical levers or stiff tethers. In the context of gravity and spinal curvature, they function as "vector sensors," detecting the exact directional pull of gravity or mechanical strain to provide high-fidelity, low-noise input to proprioceptive and bone-remodeling pathways.

## Concrete Test
**In vitro Uniaxial Strain with Truncation Mutants**
Generate truncation mutants of ACVR1 or PIEZO2 that remove the highly anisotropic domains, replacing them with flexible linkers. Culture osteoblasts or mechanosensory neurons expressing either WT or mutant forms on stretchable silicone substrates. Apply cyclic uniaxial strain and measure downstream signaling activation (e.g., SMAD1/5/8 phosphorylation for ACVR1, or calcium transients for PIEZO2).
*Prediction*: The high-anisotropy domains are required to resolve the directionality of the strain; the mutant forms will lose directional preference, resulting in noisy, isotropic signaling regardless of the stretch vector.
