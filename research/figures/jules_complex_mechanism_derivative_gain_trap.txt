Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS) is proposed to be a predictable consequence of a "Derivative Gain Trap" during the adolescent growth spurt.
During childhood, derivative gains (proprioceptive feedback via tau) provide essential damping against external perturbations.
However, during the adolescent growth spurt, the absolute neural feedback delay (tau) increases sharply as the spine elongates.
This elongation shifts the system across a critical stability margin. The once-stabilizing derivative gains become destabilizing, resulting in a supercritical Hopf bifurcation (Metabolic Buckling).
A causal loop diagram illustrating this mechanism:
1. Adolescent Growth Spurt increases Spinal Length L.
2. Increased L leads to increased Proprioceptive Delay tau.
3. As tau exceeds a critical threshold, Derivative Damping flips from Negative (Stabilizing) to Positive (Destabilizing).
4. This results in the "Derivative Gain Trap".
5. The instability initiates scoliotic curve progression, which in turn causes further mechanical asymmetry, creating a vicious cycle of structural failure.
