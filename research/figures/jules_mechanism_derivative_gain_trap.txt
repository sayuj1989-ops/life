The "Derivative Gain Trap" Mechanism:
During the adolescent growth spurt, a critical instability emerges due to a delay in the sensory feedback loop.

1.  **Normal Growth:** The spine maintains stability against gravity via a predictive control policy involving proprioception (sensors like PIEZO2) and muscle actuation. The control system uses a derivative gain ($K_D$) to dampen oscillations.
2.  **Adolescent Growth Spurt:** Spinal length ($L$) increases rapidly. This structural scaling drastically increases the neural transport delay ($\tau$) because the physical distance signals must travel increases.
3.  **The Trap:** As the delay ($\tau$) exceeds a critical threshold ($\tau_{crit}$), the previously stabilizing derivative gain ($K_D$) becomes destabilizing. The dampening force now acts out of phase with the spinal oscillations, injecting energy into the system rather than removing it.
4.  **Metabolic Buckling:** This out-of-phase feedback leads to a supercritical Hopf bifurcation. The system enters a limit cycle of expanding oscillations, which structurally manifests as scoliotic buckling (a 3D helical deformity) due to the energetic cost of control outstripping metabolic supply.
