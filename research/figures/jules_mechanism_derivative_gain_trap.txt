The "Derivative Gain Trap" during the adolescent growth spurt.
A complex causal feedback loop diagram illustrating the Biological Countercurvature theory of Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS).
The spine is an active control system governed by a Delay Differential Equation (DDE).
During childhood, derivative gains (proprioceptive feedback) provide essential damping.
During the adolescent growth spurt, neural delay (\tau) increases due to rapid spinal lengthening.
This increased delay turns previously stabilizing damping gains into destabilizing forces, causing a supercritical Hopf bifurcation.
The result is "Metabolic Buckling," initiating scoliotic curvature.
Visual elements should include a feedback loop, representations of delay (\tau) and growth, and a transition from stable damping to unstable buckling.
