The "Derivative Gain Trap" is a biomechanical feedback mechanism underlying the structural instability seen in Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS).

It describes a supercritical Hopf bifurcation scenario. During rapid adolescent spine elongation, the physical distance between sensory receptors and the central nervous system increases. This increases the neural transmission delay ($\tau$).

When this delay passes a critical instability threshold ($\tau_{crit}$), the previously stabilizing velocity feedback loop (the derivative gain, Kd, used for proprioceptive control) flips into a destabilizing force. Instead of dampening perturbations, the delayed feedback amplifies oscillatory movements, driving the spinal rod out of its normal equilibrium into unstable buckling or twisting modes.

The diagram should illustrate this control loop: a sensor sending a delayed signal $\tau$ to a controller, which applies a restoring force to a flexible spinal rod. It should visually contrast the stable regime ($\tau < \tau_{crit}$) with the unstable oscillatory regime ($\tau > \tau_{crit}$).
