The "Metabolic Buckling" mechanism, driven by the "Derivative Gain Trap" during the adolescent growth spurt, explains the onset of Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS).

During childhood, neural derivative gains provide essential damping to stabilize the spine against gravity. However, during the rapid adolescent growth spurt, the physical lengthening of the spine leads to an increase in the absolute neural feedback delay (\tau).

Due to the increased delay, these previously stabilizing derivative gains undergo a phase shift, becoming destabilizing. This triggers a supercritical Hopf bifurcation, where the body's own control system inadvertently drives the spine into a scoliotic buckling event.

This process is further exacerbated by a transient "Energy Deficit Window." The metabolic cost of maintaining a straightened spine scales geometrically (L^4), whereas metabolic supply scales with cross-sectional area (L^2). This mismatch causes localized energy insufficiency (e.g., NAD+ depletion), reducing damping capacity right when it is most needed, locking the system into the buckling trajectory.
