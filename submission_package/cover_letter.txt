I am writing to submit our manuscript, "Metabolic Buckling of the Growing Spine: An Information-Cosserat Framework for Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis," for consideration as an Original Article in Spine.

The grand challenge of the human spine is growing against gravity—a challenge that often fails during adolescence, resulting in Adolescent Idiopathic Scoliosis (AIS). While we have long understood the mechanical consequences of this failure, its root cause has remained elusive. In this manuscript, we present a breakthrough: we reclassify AIS not merely as a random genetic error, but as "metabolic buckling."

Using an Information-Cosserat mechanics framework combined with AlphaFold structural analysis, we identify a fundamental allometric scaling divergence. The structural demand of maintaining a counter-curved spine scales as $L^4$, while metabolic supply (e.g., vascular delivery and proprioceptive signaling) scales as $L^2$. This divergence creates an "Energy Deficit Window" precisely around a spine length of 0.35m, which corresponds with the typical adolescent growth spurt. In this window, the metabolic cost of maintaining the structurally anisotropic mechanosensors (such as PIEZO2 and Vimentin) required for gravity sensing becomes unaffordable, triggering a collapse into the scoliotic deformity.

While our model derives from broad biophysical principles, it is not simply a theoretical exercise for specialists. It provides a robust explanation for why current clinical standards—such as bracing—work, as they mechanically reduce the energetic demand. Crucially, our "anisotropy rescue" models demonstrate that modifying cytoskeletal stiffness can delay this buckling onset by up to 60 cm of growth, offering entirely new metabolic and pharmacological targets for early intervention. This bridges the gap between the surgical mechanics of the deformity and its underlying biological etiology, making it highly relevant to the clinical and research audience of Spine.
